[[Communication for Health education]]
**Health Education** is the process of translating scientific health knowledge into desirable individual and community behaviour patterns through educational techniques. It is not merely the delivery of information but a "two-way process" aimed at changing knowledge, attitudes, and skills.

---

### **I. Principles of Health Education** ★ Important Exam Point

The following principles are the psychological and pedagogical "rules of teaching" that ensure the success of any health education programme.

#### **Mnemonic: C-I-P M-C-R L-K-S G-F-L**

_(**C**an **I** **P**ractice **M**edicine **C**aringly **R**eaching **L**ocal **K**ind **S**ouls **G**enerating **F**ine **L**eaders?)_

---

#### **1. Credibility**

The degree to which the message is perceived as trustworthy and consistent with scientific facts. It must also be compatible with local culture and social goals.

- **Example:** A health worker advising on ORS for diarrhoea must use scientific facts that align with the community’s trust in the local clinic.

#### **2. Interest**

People are unlikely to listen to things that do not interest them. Teaching must relate to the **"felt-needs"** (needs the people feel about themselves) rather than just "health needs" defined by experts.

- **Example:** A group of villagers may not be interested in a generic "Be Healthy" slogan but will listen to a session on how to prevent the specific illnesses currently affecting their children.

#### **3. Participation**

Health education should encourage people to work actively with health workers to identify their own problems and develop solutions.

- **Example:** Involving family members in the bedside care of a patient in a hospital provides a practical opportunity for health education that the family is more likely to adopt.

#### **4. Motivation**

Motivation is the "inner power" that drives a person to learn and change. It involves two types of motives: **Primary**(sex,survival, hunger) and **Secondary** (praise, rewards).

- **Example:** Telling a woman to lose weight to prevent heart disease (primary motive) may have little effect, but telling her she will look more "charming and beautiful" (secondary motive) might motivate her to follow the advice.

#### **5. Comprehension**

The educator must know the ==level of understanding== ,==literacy==== and ==mental== ==capacity== of the audience and use *language* they understand, avoiding medical jargon.

- **Example:** A doctor asking a villager to avoid "starchy foods" may fail if the villager does not know what "starch" is; the doctor should instead name specific local foods like rice or potatoes.

#### **6. Reinforcement**

Because few people can learn everything in one session, repetition at intervals is necessary to prevent the audience from regressing to the =="pre-awareness" stage.

- **Example:** During an immunization drive, the importance of the booster dose must be repeated across multiple house visits and posters to ensure the message sticks.

#### **7. Learning by Doing**

Learning is an action-process. As the famous proverb says: _"If I hear, I forget; if I see, I remember; if I do, I know"_.

- **Example:** Instead of just talking about hand-washing, the health worker should have the children actually perform the hand-washing steps during the session.

#### **8. Known to Unknown**

Start with what the people already know and use that as a "peg" to hang new information on.

- **Example:** When teaching about the "germ theory," start by discussing the community’s existing belief that "dirt causes disease" and then introduce the concept of microscopic organisms.

#### **9. Setting an Example**

The educator must "practice what they preach" to remain credible.

- **Example:** A health worker who smokes will not be successful in teaching a group about the hazards of tobacco.

#### **10. Good Human Relations**

Sharing ideas and feelings happens most easily between people who have a good, respectful relationship.

- **Example:** A doctor who uses humour and a friendly tone will "break the ice" with a patient frozen by fear, making the education more effective.

#### **11. Feedback**

The educator must modify the message or the channel based on the reactions and suggestions of the audience.

- **Example:** If villagers stop attending a session because the time clashes with their harvest, the educator must use feedback to reschedule the sessions.

#### **12. Leaders**

We learn best from people we respect. Educators should use local leaders (teachers, village headmen) as "agents of change".

- **Example:** If a village headman is convinced to build a sanitary latrine, the rest of the community is far more likely to follow suit.

### **High-Yield Exam Points** ★

- **Felt Needs:** The cornerstone of the "Interest" principle.
- **Internalization:** The final stage of the adoption process where the new behaviour becomes part of the individual’s value system.
- **Education vs. Propaganda:** Education encourages people to think for themselves, while propaganda imposes ready-made slogans.
- **Learning Objectives:** These may be at the **Cognitive** (knowledge), **Affective** (attitudes), or **Psychomotor** (skills) levels.

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: Why is "Known to Unknown" a vital principle?**
    - **A:** It builds on the audience’s existing mental framework, making new and complex information easier to digest and less intimidating.
- **Q: What is the difference between a "Primary" and "Secondary" motive?**
    - **A:** Primary motives are inborn desires like hunger or survival; secondary motives are created by outside incentives like praise, rivalry, or rewards.
- **Q: How does "Learning by Doing" differ from "Reinforcement"?**
    - **A:** Learning by doing focuses on the _method_ of acquiring a skill through action; reinforcement focuses on the _repetition_ of the message over time to ensure it is not forgotten.

---

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Confusing Information with Education:** Information is just a transfer of facts; education is a process aimed at **behaviour change**.
- **Ignoring the Educator’s Behaviour:** Forgetting that "Setting an Example" is a formal principle. If the educator doesn't follow their own advice, the entire programme fails.
- **Using Jargon in Examples:** When asked for examples of "Comprehension," students often use technical terms. Use simple language as the source suggests (e.g., "rice" instead of "carbohydrates").
