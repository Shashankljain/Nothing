[[Health programmes in India]]
**Blood safety** is a critical component of public health and modern medicine, ensuring that patients receive blood or blood products that are free from infectious agents and are immunologically compatible. In India, the **Blood Safety Programme**was launched in **1989** as part of the National AIDS Control Programme to prevent the transmission of HIV and other blood-borne infections.

---

### **I. Definition of Blood Safety**

Blood safety refers to the entire process of ensuring that blood and its components (like platelets, plasma, and factors) are ==safe for transfusion.== It includes:

- **Preventing Transfusion-Transmitted Infections (TTIs):** Ensuring blood is free from pathogens like HIV, Hepatitis, and Malaria.
- **Quality Management:** Implementing rigorous standards for collection, testing, storage, and transport (immuno-hematology and logistics).

---

### **II. Why Blood Safety is Necessary (Etiology of TTIs)**

Blood acts as a potent **vehicle** for the transmission of various infectious agents. If blood is not properly screened, the risk of infection to the recipient is extremely high (e.g., the risk of contracting HIV from an infected unit of blood is estimated to be over **95%**).

**Common Transfusion-Transmitted Infections (TTIs):**

- **Viral:** HIV 1 & 2, Hepatitis B (HBV), Hepatitis C (HCV), Cytomegalovirus (CMV), and Infectious Mononucleosis.
- **Bacterial/Spirochetal:** Syphilis and Brucellosis.
- **Protozoal:** Malaria and Trypanosomiasis (Chaga’s disease).

---

### **III. How to Ensure Blood Safety (Strategies and Mechanism)**

The Government of India, through the **National AIDS Control Organisation (NACO)** and the **Central Drug Standard Control Organization (CDSCO)**, ensures blood safety through a multi-tiered approach.

#### **1. Mandatory Screening of Every Unit** ★ Important Exam Point

It is legally mandatory to test every single unit of donated blood for the following five infections before it is released for transfusion:

1. **HIV 1 & 2**
2. **Hepatitis B (HBsAg)**
3. **Hepatitis C (HCV)**
4. **Malaria**
5. **Syphilis**

#### **2. Technological and Infrastructure Strengthening**

- **Zonal Blood Testing Centres:** Established to centralize and standardise testing.
- **Blood Component Separation Units (BCSU):** Promoting the use of components (platelets, plasma) rather than whole blood, which allows one unit of blood to help multiple patients and reduces volume-related complications.
- **Quality Management:** Implementing standard operating procedures (SOPs) and external quality assessment schemes.
- **Cold Chain Maintenance:** Ensuring blood is stored and transported at appropriate temperatures (similar to vaccines) to prevent spoilage or bacterial growth.

#### **3. Regulatory Oversight**

The **CDSCO** acts as the Central License Approving Authority for all blood banks and blood products in India, ensuring they meet the requirements of the **Drugs and Cosmetics Act**.

---

### **IV. Current Policy Regarding Blood Donation** ★ Important Exam Point

The national blood policy has shifted significantly to ensure a safe and sustainable supply of blood.

|**Policy Component**|**Details**|
|:--|:--|
|**Prohibition of Paid Donors**|**Professional/paid blood donation has been prohibited** in India since **1st January 1998**.|
|**Voluntary Donation**|The policy focuses on achieving **100% voluntary, non-remunerated blood donation**.|
|**Donor Selection**|High-risk groups (e.g., professional sex workers, injecting drug users, or those with multiple partners) are urged to refrain from donating blood.|
|**Licensing**|Only **licensed blood banks** are permitted to operate and collect blood in the country.|
|**Decentralization**|Establishment of blood storage centres at **First Referral Units (FRUs)** at sub-district levels for emergency needs like obstetric care and trauma.|

---

### **V. Who Can/Cannot Donate? (Donor Eligibility)**

- **Rejection Criteria:** Donors who test positive for **HBsAg (Australia Antigen)** or HIV must be rejected and told not to share personal items like razors or toothbrushes.
- **High-Risk Groups:** Individuals with a history of intravenous drug use or high-risk sexual behaviour are excluded to protect the "safe blood" pool.

---

### **VI. Public Health Significance**

Blood safety is not just about individual transfusions; it is a vital indicator of health service quality. In the context of **Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)**, the proportion of blood units screened for TTIs is a key indicator for progress toward ending epidemics of viral hepatitis and AIDS by 2030.

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Prohibition Date:** Professional blood donation was banned on **Jan 1, 1998**.
- **Mandatory Tests:** HIV, HBV, HCV, Malaria, and Syphilis.

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: Why was professional blood donation banned?**
    - **A:** Paid donors are more likely to hide high-risk behaviours or infections to receive payment, significantly increasing the risk of Transfusion-Transmitted Infections (TTIs).
- **Q: What is "window period" and how does it affect blood safety?**
    - **A:** It is the time between infection and the appearance of detectable antibodies (e.g., 2–12 weeks for HIV). During this time, a donor is highly infectious but may test negative on standard antibody tests.
- **Q: What is a BCSU?**
    - **A:** Blood Component Separation Unit. It separates whole blood into components like Packed RBCs, Fresh Frozen Plasma, and Platelets.

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Mixing up the tests:** Forgetting to mention Syphilis or Malaria in the mandatory list of five tests.
- **Incorrect year:** Stating that the ban on paid donors started with the 1989 programme (it actually started in 1998).
- **Incomplete definition:** Defining blood safety only as "screening for HIV" rather than including quality management, logistics, and donor selection.
