Growth Monitoring in Children

1. Introduction and Definition

Growth monitoring is the process of sequential, periodic measurements of physical growth (most commonly weight-for-age) to assess the health and nutritional status of a child. It is a continuous surveillance tool used to detect early signs of "growth failure" before a child becomes clinically ill.

- ★ Definition: It is the regular recording and interpretation of a child's physical growth, providing a visual record of growth progress over time.
- The Primary Tool: The Growth Chart, also known as the "Road-to-Health" chart (first designed by David Morley and later modified by WHO), is the visible display used for longitudinal follow-up.

2. Objectives of Growth Monitoring ★
## PROTECT

According to the source material, growth monitoring has seven key objectives and uses:

1. Growth Monitoring: To provide a visual, longitudinal record of a child's growth progress.
2. Diagnostic Tool: To identify "high-risk" children (those with growth failure) early, long before clinical signs and symptoms of malnutrition become apparent.
3. Planning and Policy Making: By grading malnutrition (e.g., normal vs. underweight), it provides an objective basis for health care planning at the local and central levels.
4. Educational Tool: Due to its visual nature, the mother can be educated on her child's health and encouraged to participate actively in monitoring her child’s growth.
5. Tool for Action: It helps the health worker determine the specific type of intervention needed (e.g., supplementary feeding, medical referral).
6. Evaluation: It provides a method to evaluate the effectiveness of corrective measures or the impact of specific health programmes on child development.
7. Tool for Teaching: It can be used to teach parents about the importance of adequate feeding and the negative effects of diseases like diarrhoea.

8. Methodology: How and When to Monitor

A. Measurement Parameter

- Weight-for-Age: This is considered the best single parameter for assessing physical growth. While a single weight record shows the child's size at that moment, sequential measurements show the rate of growth.

B. Frequency of Weighing ★

Measurement should be repeated at the following intervals:

- Birth to 1 year: Monthly.
- 1 to 2 years: Every two months.
- 2 to 5 years: Every three months.

4. Interpretation of the Growth Curve ★

The direction of the curve on the growth chart is more important than the position of any single dot.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Curve Direction|Interpretation|Clinical Significance|
|Rising Curve|Normal Growth|Indicates the child is gaining weight and is healthy.|
|Flat Curve (Stationary)|Growth Failure|The earliest sign of protein-energy malnutrition (PEM).|
|Downward Curve|Serious Condition|Indicates a dangerous loss of weight; requires urgent medical investigation.|

5. Identification of "At-Risk" Infants ★

One major objective of growth monitoring is to identify "at-risk" children who require special intensive care. Basic criteria include:

1. Birth weight less than 2.5 kg.
2. Twins.
3. Birth order 5 and more.
4. Artificial feeding.
5. Weight below 70% of expected weight (Grade II and III malnutrition).
6. Failure to gain weight during three successive months.
7. Children with PEM, diarrhoea, or measles.
8. Working mother or single parent.

9. Growth Indicators Summary

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Indicator|Definition/Cut-off|Reflects|
|Weight-for-age|Comparison of weight against age standards.|Overall growth and nutrition.|
|Height-for-age|Low height for age is called Stunting.|Past or chronic malnutrition.|
|Weight-for-height|Low weight for height is called Wasting.|Acute malnutrition (emaciation).|

High-Yield Exam Points ★

- The "Passport": The growth chart is often described as a "passport to child health care".
- Flattening Curve: This is the earliest sign of protein-energy malnutrition (PEM) and may precede clinical signs by weeks.
- WHO Standards: India adopted the New WHO Child Growth Standards (2006) in February 2009 for use in the ICDS and NRHM programmes.
- Reference Standards: The WHO 2006 reference values are now the standard method of monitoring children's health and nutritional status globally.

Viva Questions and Answers

Q1: What is the "Road-to-Health" chart? A: It is a growth chart designed to visually monitor a child's physical growth and development over time to detect malnutrition early.

Q2: Why is the direction of the curve more important than a single dot? A: A single dot only shows the child's size at one moment. The direction of the curve (trend) shows whether the child is gaining, maintaining, or losing weight, which reflects the rate of growth and health status.

Q3: What does a flat growth curve indicate? A: It indicates growth failure and is the earliest indicator of protein-energy malnutrition.

Q4: How often should a 6-month-old be weighed for growth monitoring? A: Ideally monthly until the age of one year.

One-Page Exam Summary

- Definition: Regular measurement (weight-for-age) to detect growth deviations.
- Tool: Road-to-Health Chart (Morley/WHO).
- Objectives: Diagnostic (early PEM detection), Educational (mother's participation), Action (intervention), and Evaluation.
- Frequency: Monthly (0-1 yr), 2-monthly (1-2 yrs), 3-monthly (2-5 yrs).
- Interpretations: Rising (Healthy), Flat (Growth Failure/PEM), Falling (Danger).
- Key Indicators: Stunting (Height-for-age), Wasting (Weight-for-height).
- At-Risk criteria: LBW (<2.5kg), twins, birth order 5+, malnourished, and stationary weight for 3 months.
