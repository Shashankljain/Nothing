[[RNTCP]]
Under the **National Tuberculosis Elimination Programme (NTEP)**—formerly known as the **Revised National Tuberculosis Control Programme (RNTCP)**—early identification of "Presumptive TB" cases is the most vital component of the control strategy. The program has transitioned from a purely passive case-finding approach to more intensified and active strategies to achieve elimination targets.

---

### **I. Primary Case-Finding Tool: Sputum Smear Microscopy**

Sputum smear examination by direct microscopy is considered the **method of choice** for TB case-finding globally.

#### **1. Why it is the Gold Standard for Case Finding:**

- **Reliability & Ease:** It is inexpensive, relatively easy to perform, and highly reliable.
- **Public Health Impact:** It identifies the most infectious cases (those excreting bacilli), who contribute most to the "pool of infection".
- **Predictive Value:** A positive smear indicates at least 10,000 organisms per ml of sputum.

#### **2. Collection of Sputum Samples** ★ Important Exam Point

A pulmonary TB suspect should submit **two sputum samples**.

- **Day 1 (Sample 1):** An "on-the-spot" sample collected under supervision when the patient first presents to the health facility.
- **Day 2 (Sample 2):** An "early morning" sample collected by the patient at home. This is more likely to contain a higher concentration of bacilli because secretions build up in the airways overnight.
- _Note:_ If the patient has come from a long distance, two "spot" specimens may be collected one hour apart.

#### **3. Microscopy Techniques Used:**

- **Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) Staining:** The traditional method using a conventional light microscope to identify Acid-Fast Bacilli (AFB).
- **LED Fluorescence Microscopy (LED-FM):** Now recommended as a superior alternative. It is faster (scanning takes 1–2 minutes) and has a 5–10 times larger field of view.

#### **4. Grading of Sputum Smear (Slide Reporting)**

|Number of Bacilli Seen|Result|Grading|
|:--|:--|:--|
|0 AFB per 100 oil immersion fields|Negative|0|
|1–9 AFB per 100 oil immersion fields|Positive|Scanty (record exact number)|
|10–99 AFB per 100 oil immersion fields|Positive|+ (1+)|
|1–10 AFB per oil immersion field|Positive|++ (2+)|

### **II. Modern Upfront Tool: Rapid Molecular Tests (NAAT)**

The program has shifted to offering **upfront Nucleic Acid Amplification Testing (NAAT)**, such as **CBNAAT (GeneXpert)** or **Truenat**, for prioritized groups (PLHIV, children, extra-pulmonary cases, and smear-negative suspects).

- **Function:** It detects DNA sequences specific to _M. tuberculosis_ and tests for **Rifampicin resistance**simultaneously.
- **Turnaround Time:** Results are available in approximately 90 minutes.

---

### **III. Supportive Tools**

- **Chest X-Ray:** Used as a screening tool. It is particularly helpful for diagnosing smear-negative pulmonary TB and TB in children, though it is not routinely indicated for smear-positive cases.
- **Tuberculin Skin Test (Mantoux):** Primarily used for prevalence estimation in a population rather than individual diagnosis of active disease.

---

### **IV. Note on False Results in Microscopy**

#### **1. Reasons for False Positivity** ★ Important Exam Point

A false positive means the result is reported as positive even though the patient does not have TB.

- **Red Stain Retention:** Scratches on the slide may retain the red stain.
- **Contamination:** Use of a slide contaminated with environmental mycobacteria or accidental transfer of AFB from a positive slide to a negative one during processing.
- **Non-AFB Particles:** Presence of food particles, precipitates, or other microorganisms that are acid-fast.

#### **2. Reasons for False Negativity** ★ Important Exam Point

A false negative means the result is reported as negative despite the patient having TB.

- **Collection Errors:** Inadequate sample volume, inappropriate containers, or storing the sputum for too long before testing.
- **Processing Errors:** Faulty sampling of the sputum for the smear, or incorrect preparation and staining of the slide.
- **Interpretation Errors:** Inadequate time spent examining the slide (a technician should examine 200 fields before declaring it negative) or visual fatigue.
- **Administrative Errors:** Misidentification of patients or incorrect labelling of samples.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Presumptive TB definition:** Any person with symptoms suggestive of TB (most commonly a persistent cough for $\ge$ 2 weeks).
- **Sample Requirement:** Two samples are essential; one positive specimen is enough to declare a patient "Smear Positive".
- **Microscopist Workload:** To maintain quality, one microscopist should not examine more than 20 slides per day.

---

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: Why is an early morning sample better than a spot sample?**
    - **A:** Bacilli and secretions accumulate in the bronchial tree during sleep, leading to a higher concentration of organisms in the first expectoration of the morning.
- **Q: What is the significance of the "Cycle Threshold" (CT) value in RT-PCR?**
    - **A:** (Context of COVID/Molecular TB tests) A lower CT value implies a higher viral/bacterial load because fewer cycles were needed to detect the material.
- **Q: Can a Mantoux test confirm active TB?**
    - **A:** No, it only indicates past or present infection. A positive result does not necessarily mean the person has active disease.

---

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Mixing up samples:** Forgetting that the standard is **one spot and one morning** sample, not two spot samples (unless default is likely).
- **Fields examined:** Stating a slide is negative after only a few fields; 200 fields must be scanned.
- **Workload:** Overestimating how many slides a technician can accurately read in a day.
