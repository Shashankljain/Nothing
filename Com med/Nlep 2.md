[[NLEP]]
The **National Leprosy Eradication Programme (NLEP)** is a centrally sponsored health scheme in India. It was launched in **1983** as a continuation of the National Leprosy Control Programme with the primary goal of eradicating leprosy from the country by introducing **Multidrug Therapy (MDT)**.

### **I. Aim and Objectives**

The basic goal of the programme is to reduce the prevalence of leprosy to such a low level that it is no longer a public health problem.

**Core Objectives:** ★ Important Exam Point

- **Interrupt Transmission:** Sterilise infectious patients as rapidly as possible using bactericidal drugs.
- **Early Detection:** Identify and treat cases early to prevent physical deformities and disabilities.
- **Prevent Drug Resistance:** Use a combination of drugs (MDT) to prevent the emergence of resistant strains.
- **Reduce Stigma:** Lower the level of social stigma associated with leprosy.
- **Medical Rehabilitation:** Provide disability prevention and medical rehabilitation for affected persons.

---

### **II. Strategy of NLEP**

The programme shifted from a "camp-based" approach to an **Integrated Leprosy Services** approach, meaning leprosy services are now delivered through the general health care system.

1. **Intensification of Case Detection:** Done through population surveys (if prevalence is high), school surveys, contact information, and voluntary referral.
2. **Regular Treatment with MDT:** MDT is the backbone of the programme, designed to increase compliance and be cost-effective.
3. **Accompanied MDT (A-MDT):** If a patient cannot visit the clinic, a responsible person (family/friend) can collect the full course of treatment for them.
4. **Disability Prevention and Medical Rehabilitation (DPMR):** Focused on preventing the transition from impairment to handicap.
5. **Information, Education, and Communication (IEC):** Intensified efforts to educate the community about the curability of the disease.

---

### **III. Classification of Leprosy for Treatment** ★ Important Exam Point

Under NLEP/WHO guidelines, patients are categorized to determine the correct treatment regimen:

|Feature|Paucibacillary (PB)|Multibacillary (MB)|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Skin Lesions**|1 to 5 lesions|6 or more lesions|
|**Nerve Involvement**|Only 1 nerve|More than 1 nerve|
|**Skin Smear**|Negative at all sites|Positive at any site|

### **IV. Treatment Regimens (WHO-MDT)** ★ Important Exam Point

The programme provides free treatment. MDT combines drugs to kill the bacilli and prevent relapse.

|Type of Leprosy|Drugs Used|Adult Dose|Duration|Follow-up|
|:--|:--|:--|:--|:--|
|**Multibacillary (MB)**|Rifampicin, Dapsone, Clofazimine|Rifampicin: 600mg (Monthly-Sup)Dapsone: 100mg (Daily-Self)Clofazimine: 300mg (Monthly-Sup) + 50mg (Daily-Self)|**12 Months**(extendable to 18)|Once a year for 5 years|
|**Paucibacillary (PB)**|Rifampicin, Dapsone|Rifampicin: 600mg (Monthly-Sup)Dapsone: 100mg (Daily-Self)|**6 Months**|Once a year for 2 years|
|**Single Skin Lesion**|ROM (Rifampicin, Ofloxacin, Minocycline)|R: 600mg, O: 400mg, M: 100mg|**Single Dose**|(Currently treated as PB)|

### **V. Organizational Structure**

NLEP follows a decentralized, multi-tier structure to ensure reach into rural areas.

- **National Level:** NLEP Commissioner (Planning/Policy) under the Director-General of Health Services.
- **State Level:** State NLEP Officer and Directorate of Health Services.
- **District/Regional Level:** District and Zonal Leprosy Officers.
- **Peripheral Implementation Units:**
    - **Leprosy Control Unit:** Established where prevalence is >1% (covers 4.5 lakh population).
    - **SET Centers (Survey, Education, Training):** Attached to PHCs for populations of 20,000–25,000 in low-endemic areas.
    - **Urban Leprosy Unit:** Covers urban populations of ~100,000.
    - **Mobile Leprosy Treatment Units:** Provide services in non-endemic areas.

### **VII. Prevention of Deformities (WHO Grading)**

Deformities are common in leprosy (affecting ~1/4th of untreated patients). NLEP uses the following grading to monitor disability:

**Hands and Feet:**

- **Grade 0:** No anesthesia, no visible deformity.
- **Grade I:** Anesthesia present but no visible deformity.
- **Grade II:** Visible deformity (e.g., claw hand, foot drop).

**Eyes:**

- **Grade 0:** No eye problem/vision loss.
- **Grade I:** Eye problem due to leprosy but no vision loss.
- **Grade II:** Severe visual impairment (vision <6/60), lagophthalmos, or corneal opacity.

### **VIII. Achievements**

- **Interruption of wild virus:** India interrupted wild leprosy transmission in 15 states by December 2005.
- **Cure Rates:** Achieved cure rates of 90% for MB and 95% for PB cases in 2011.
- **Prevalence:** Significant reduction to 0.68 per 10,000 population by 2011.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points** ★

- **MDT Components:** Rifampicin is the most important bactericidal drug; Clofazimine helps prevent leprosy reactions.
- **ASHA Role:** ASHAs act as the bridge between the village and health system, assisting in case detection and treatment adherence.
- **Elimination vs. Eradication:** NLEP aimed for "elimination" (PR <1/10,000) as a milestone toward eventual eradication.

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: Why is Clofazimine added in MB leprosy?**
    - **A:** It is a weak bactericidal drug but has valuable anti-inflammatory properties that help manage "Lepra Reactions".
- **Q: What is "Accompanied MDT"?**
    - **A:** It is a strategy to ensure treatment completion for patients who cannot visit clinics regularly by giving the full drug supply to a guardian.
- **Q: What is the criteria for a "Relapse" in leprosy?**
    - **A:** Reappearance of the disease after the patient was declared cured following a full course of MDT.

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Confusing PB and MB criteria:** Remember, MB starts at **6** lesions, not 5.
- **Duration of treatment:** Students often swap the 6-month (PB) and 12-month (MB) durations.
- **Grading:** Forgetting that Grade I is anesthesia _only_, while Grade II involves _visible_ changes like clawing or ulcers.
