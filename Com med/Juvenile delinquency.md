**Juvenile delinquency** is a critical social pathology where individuals under the age of majority exhibit behavior that deviates from legal and social norms. In the context of Community Medicine, it is viewed not just as a legal issue but as a reflection of the social and psychological health of the community.

---

### **I. Introduction and Definition**

- **Definition:** A juvenile delinquent is defined as a minor who shows a deviation from normal behavior and has committed an offense punishable by law, such as theft, sexual offenses, murder, or burglary.
- **Social Perspective:** It is recognized as a major **social problem** arising from the failure of an individual to adjust to their environment.
- **Adolescent Context:** The transition from adolescence to adulthood is often a "stormy" period; failure to satisfy basic needs during this phase often manifests as delinquency.

---

### **II. Etiology: Why does it happen? (Causes)** ★ Important Exam Point

The causes of juvenile delinquency are complex and multifactorial, involving an interaction between the individual, their family, and the wider society.

|**Category**|**Specific Factors**|
|:--|:--|
|**Family & Social Factors**|**Broken Homes:** Separation or death of parents leads to "mental deprivation," a potent pathogenic factor.  **Problem Families:** Families characterized by poverty, backwardness, and marital disharmony. **Lack of Affection:** Rejection or neglect by parents during the first 5 years of life.|
|**Environmental Factors**|**Urbanization & Industrialization:** Movement to impersonal city environments and overcrowding. **Slum Living:** Proximity to crime-prone areas and lack of recreational facilities.  **Mass Media:** Exposure to violence and unrealistic economic aspirations through cinema and TV.|
|**Behavioural Factors**|**Drug Addiction & Alcoholism:** Substance abuse is both a cause and a consequence of delinquency. **Peer Pressure:** Association with "gangs" or antisocial subcultures.|
|**Psychological Factors**|**Emotional Instability:** Feelings of insecurity, anxiety, or inferiority.  **Failure to adjust:** Inability to cope with the "stormy" psychological transitions of adolescence.|

---

### **III. Preventive and Control Measures** ★ Important Exam Point

Management involves a "Social Defence" approach, which focuses on prevention, therapy, and rehabilitation rather than just punishment.

#### **1. The Children Act, 1960**

The primary legal framework in India for the prevention and control of juvenile delinquency is **The Children Act, 1960**. It provides a specialized approach to the care, protection, maintenance, and training of delinquent children.

#### **2. Institutional Infrastructure**

The Act established specific institutions to handle delinquent youth away from the adult criminal justice system:

- **Juvenile/Children’s Courts:** To hear cases in a child-friendly environment.
- **Child Welfare Boards:** To decide on the best interest of the child.
- **Remand Homes:** For temporary stay while the case is being processed.
- **Certified Schools:** For the long-term education and vocational training of delinquents.
- **After-care Facilities:** To help the child reintegrate into society after release.

#### **3. Social Defence Mechanisms**

- **National Institute of Social Defence (1975):** The nodal agency under the Department of Social Welfare that coordinates correctional services.
- **Healthy Growth Environment:** Creating community conditions conducive to wholesome human life to prevent the onset of criminality.
- **Juvenile Justice Act:** Subsequent legislation (1986 and onwards) further refined these protections.

#### **4. Community-Based Interventions**

- **Education:** Teaching parents about child-rearing and the importance of a stable home life.
- **Mental Health Services:** Utilizing child guidance clinics and psychiatric services for children with emotional problems.
- **Rehabilitation:** Coordination between health, education, and social welfare departments to restore the child’s functional ability.

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Delinquency is a Social Pathology:** It is linked to poverty, broken homes, and "problem families".
- **The "Stormy" Phase:** Adolescence (10–19 years) is the most vulnerable period for the development of delinquent behavior.
- **The Children Act, 1960:** Must be mentioned as the core legislative measure in India.
- **Social Defence:** Focuses on creating a "healthy and wholesome growth" environment rather than merely treating the offended.

---

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: What is a "Problem Family"?**
    - **A:** A family that lags behind the rest of the community due to backwardness, poverty, instability, and marital disharmony, often producing victims of delinquency.
- **Q: How does a "Remand Home" differ from a jail?**
    - **A:** A Remand Home is a protective facility for temporary stay during investigation, focusing on care rather than incarceration.
- **Q: Why is "After-care" important?**
    - **A:** It ensures that once a juvenile is released, they have the social and vocational support to prevent a relapse into criminal behavior.

---

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Confusing Juvenile Delinquency with Adult Crime:** Students often forget that the legal approach for juveniles focuses on **rehabilitation**, not punishment.
- **Ignoring Social Factors:** Many focus only on the legal definition and forget to discuss the role of **broken homes**and **poverty** as root causes.
- **Missing the Act:** Failing to mention **The Children Act, 1960** or the **Juvenile Justice Act** significantly reduces marks.
