[[Health programmes in India]]

The **National Cancer Control Programme (NCCP)** is a vital public health initiative in India aimed at **==reducing the morbidity==** and ==mortality== associated with cancer through primary prevention, early detection, and improved treatment facilities.

---

### **I. Introduction and Milestones**

The NCCP was first launched by the Government of India in **1975–76**. Over the decades, it has undergone several revisions to expand its scope and integrate with other non-communicable disease (NCD) efforts.

**Key Milestones:**

- **1975–76:** Launch of the National Cancer Control Programme.
- **1984–85:** The strategy was revised to emphasize primary prevention and early detection of cancer.
- **1992:** Integration of various cancer control activities.
- **2010:** The programme was integrated into a larger framework called the **National Programme for Prevention and Control of Cancer, Diabetes, Cardiovascular Diseases and Stroke (NPCDCS)**.
- **2017:** National Health Policy (NHP) set new targets to reduce premature mortality from NCDs, including cancer.

---

### **II. Aim and Objectives**

The ultimate goal is to reduce the ==burden of cancer== by preventing its occurrence and improving survival rates.

**Core Objectives:** ★ Important Exam Point

1. **Primary Prevention:** To prevent cancer by controlling ==risk factors== (e.g., tobacco, alcohol) and ==health education,promoting healthy lifestyles.==
2. **Secondary Prevention:** To ==detect and treat== cancer at an early, pre-malignant, or in situ stage through screening.
3. **Tertiary Prevention:** To provide ==comprehensive treatment== and palliative care to those with advanced disease.
4. **Strengthen Infrastructure:** To develop and expand the network of *==Regional Cancer Centres (RCCs) and oncology wings==* in medical colleges.
5. **Surveillance:** To maintain a database through ==Cancer Registries== (Hospital and Population-based).

---

### **III. Public Health Significance (Magnitude of the Problem)**

- **Global Burden:** In 2022, there were an estimated 19.9 million new cancer cases globally.
- **India Scenario:** In 2022, India reported approximately **1.41 million new cases** and **916,827 deaths**.
- **Top Cancers in India:**
    - **Males:** Lip/Oral cavity, Lung, Oesophagus.
    - **Females:** Breast, Cervix uteri, Ovary.
- **Productive Years:** NCDs (including cancer) account for significant loss in potentially productive years (ages 35–64).

### **V. Cancer Screening Strategies** ★ Important Exam Point

Screening is the "search for unrecognized malignancy" in apparently healthy people.

|Cancer Type|Target Group|Recommended Screening Method|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Cervical**|Women aged 30–65|Pap Smear, VIA (Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid), or HPV-DNA testing.|
|**Breast**|All adult women|Breast Self-Examination (BSE), Clinical Palpation, Mammography (for high-risk/older).|
|**Oral**|Tobacco/Alcohol users|Visual inspection of the oral cavity by health workers during home visits.|

---

### **VI. Organizational Structure and Service Delivery**

The programme operates through a tiered system to ensure reach:

1. **Tertiary Level:** **Regional Cancer Centres (RCCs)** and Centres of Excellence provide advanced research, professional leadership, and complex surgeries.
2. **Secondary Level:** Oncology wings in medical colleges and District Hospitals manage common cancers and follow-up.
3. **Primary Level:** **Health and Wellness Centres (HWCs)** and PHCs are responsible for IEC (Information, Education, Communication), screening, and referral.

---

### **VII. Indicators Used**

- **NCD Mortality:** Reduction in premature mortality (ages 30–70).
- **Cancer Incidence Rate:** Number of new cases per lakh population.
- **Screening Coverage:** Percentage of the target population screened for cervical, breast, and oral cancers.

---

### **VIII. Recent Updates and 2030 Targets**

- **NHP 2017 Goal:** Achieve a **25% relative reduction** in premature mortality from cancer and other NCDs by 2025.
- **HPV Update:** WHO now encourages objective **HPV-DNA testing** every 5 to 10 years as it is more cost-effective than Pap smears in limited-resource settings.
- **Ayushman Bharat:** Financial risk protection for cancer treatment is provided through the **Jan Arogya Yojana**.

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Danger Signals of Cancer (Table 8):** 1. Lump in breast, 2. Change in a mole, 3. Persistent bowel/bladder changes, 4. Persistent cough/hoarseness, 5. Excessive menstrual/abnormal bleeding, 6. Blood from any orifice, 7. Non-healing sore, 8. Unexplained weight loss. ★ Important Exam Point
- **Iceberg Phenomenon:** Hospital data only represents the "tip of the iceberg"; many cases remain undiagnosed in the community.
- **VIA Method:** 5% acetic acid is used to identify opaque acetowhite lesions.

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: Why is VIA preferred over Pap Smear in rural India?**
    - **A:** VIA is easy to carry out, requires minimal laboratory equipment, and allows for immediate results and a "single-visit" approach to management.
- **Q: What is the most effective way to prevent oral cancer?**
    - **A:** Primary prevention through the elimination of tobacco habits (smoking, chewing).
- **Q: Who is a "high-risk" individual for breast cancer?**
    - **A:** Women with a family history of breast cancer, late age at first birth, or early menarche/late menopause.

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Confusing NCCP and NPCDCS:** Remember that the NCCP is now a component of the integrated NPCDCS.
- **Ignoring Primary Prevention:** Students often focus only on surgery/chemo; examiners want to hear about **tobacco control** and **vaccination**.
- **Incorrect Years:** Mixing up the original launch (1975) with the major revision/NCD integration (2010).
