[[Health programmes in India]]
The **Revised National Tuberculosis Control Programme (RNTCP)** was launched in 1993 to address the failure of the earlier National Tuberculosis Programme (NTP, 1962). It was revitalized using the WHO-recommended **DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course)** strategy.

In 2020, in line with the goal of ending TB by 2025, the programme was renamed the **National Tuberculosis Elimination Programme (NTEP)**.

---

### **Objectives of the Programme**

The initial objectives of RNTCP were focused on standardising treatment and ensuring high cure rates:

1. **Achievement of at least 85% cure rate** of infectious (sputum-positive) cases of tuberculosis through DOTS.
2. **Augmentation of case-finding activities** to detect at least 70% of estimated cases using quality sputum microscopy.

Under the current **NTEP (National Strategic Plan 2017–2025)**, the objectives have evolved towards "Elimination":

- **Find:** Reach all TB patients, including those seeking care in the private sector and undiagnosed cases in high-risk populations.
- **Treat:** Initiate and sustain all patients on appropriate anti-TB treatment regardless of where they seek care.
- **Prevent:** Prevent the emergence of TB in susceptible populations and high-risk groups.
- **Build:** Strengthen enabling policies and human resources with enhanced capacities.

---

### **Strategies of RNTCP/NTEP**

The programme has transitioned through three major strategic frameworks:

#### **1. The DOTS Strategy (Core Strategy)**

Initially, RNTCP was built on five main pillars:

- **Political Will and Administrative Commitment:** Ensuring sustained funding and planning.
- **Diagnosis by Sputum Smear Microscopy:** Reliable and early diagnosis at the periphery.
- **Regular Supply of Quality Drugs:** Use of patient-wise boxes to prevent stock-outs.
- **Directly Observed Treatment:** Ensuring drugs are swallowed in the presence of a provider (DOT agent).
- **Systematic Monitoring and Accountability:** Using standardised records and reports.

#### **2. The Stop TB Strategy (2006 onwards)**

Added new dimensions to DOTS:

- Addressing TB/HIV co-infection and MDR-TB.
- Contributing to health system strengthening.
- Engaging all care providers (Public-Private Mix).
- Empowering people with TB and communities.

#### **3. The End TB Strategy (Current)**

Guided by three pillars to reach **90-90-90 targets** (90% reach, 90% key populations, 90% treatment success):

- **Pillar 1:** Integrated, patient-centred care and prevention (includes early diagnosis and universal DST).
- **Pillar 2:** Bold policies and supportive systems (Universal Health Coverage, social protection).
- **Pillar 3:** Intensified research and innovation (new tools, vaccines).

---

### **Strengths of the Programme**

1. **Decentralized Care:** Services are integrated into the general health system, making treatment accessible through PHCs and sub-centres.
2. **Free Services:** Quality-assured diagnostics (like CBNAAT) and anti-TB drugs are provided free of charge to all patients.
3. **Nikshay IT System:** A web-based application for real-time tracking of TB patients, notification, and treatment adherence.
4. **Daily Drug Regimen:** Transitioned from thrice-weekly intermittent doses to **Daily Fixed-Dose Combinations (FDCs)**, which simplifies treatment and improves compliance.
5. **Universal DST:** The goal is to provide **Universal Drug Susceptibility Testing** to all TB patients upfront to detect drug resistance early.
6. **Incentive Schemes:** Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) schemes like **Nikshay Poshan Yojana** provide ₹500/month for nutritional support to patients.

---

### **Weaknesses and Challenges**

1. **Drug-Resistant TB (MDR/XDR-TB):** India has the highest absolute number of MDR-TB cases, which are difficult and expensive to treat.
2. **Private Sector Engagement:** A significant portion of patients (approx. 70%) seek care in the private sector, where notification and adherence to standard protocols have historically been low.
3. **TB-HIV Co-infection:** HIV increases the risk of progression to active TB, and TB is the leading cause of death among PLHIV.
4. **Social Determinants:** Malnutrition, overcrowding, shanty housing, and poverty remain "non-specific" determinants that impede the rapid conquest of the disease.
5. **Social Stigma:** Patients often delay seeking care or disclose their status late due to fear of discrimination.

---

### **★ High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Diagnostic Gold Standard:** While microscopy was the earlier standard, the programme now prioritises **upfront NAAT (CBNAAT/TrueNat)** for all presumptive TB cases.
- **NTEP Goal:** India aims to eliminate TB by **2025**, five years ahead of the Global SDG target of 2030.
- **Treatment Phases:** Intensive Phase (IP) involves 4 drugs for 8 weeks; Continuation Phase (CP) involves 3 drugs for 16 weeks.
- **Nikshay:** Remember this keyword for any question on TB surveillance in India.
