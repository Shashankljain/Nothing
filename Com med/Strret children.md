[[PM in Paediatrics]]

**Street children** represent a significant social and public health challenge in the field of Community Medicine. They are often categorized under **"Social Pathology"** because their condition is inextricably linked to poverty, family disintegration, and the failure of social support systems.

---

### **I. Introduction and Context**

- **Definition:** While not strictly defined by age alone in the sources, street children are generally minors boys and girls who live and/or work on the streets, often lacking parental supervision or a permanent home.
- **Social Perspective:** They are viewed as a product of **urbanization** and **industrialization**, where the "warmth of the village community" is replaced by the impersonal and often harsh environment of the city.
- **Vulnerability:** They are classified as a "bridge population" and a "vulnerable group" due to their lack of access to health information and preventive measures.

### **II. Etiology: Why are children on the street?** ★ Important Exam Point

The presence of children on the street is rarely due to a single cause; it is a multifactorial issue.

1. **Broken Homes:** The death of parents, separation, or divorce leads to "mental deprivation," which is a major pathogenic factor. Children from such homes often go astray in search of happiness or survival.
2. **Problem Families:** Families characterized by backwardness, poverty, instability, and marital disharmony often "lag behind" the community, forcing children into the streets.
3. **Urban Migration:** The wave of migration from rural to urban areas often results in the loss of traditional family support systems, leaving children unsupervised.
4. **Economic Aspirations:** Mass media often encourages unrealistic economic goals, which, when coupled with poverty, may drive children to seek "easy money" through illicit activities on the street.

---

### **III. Health Risks and Pathologies** ★ Important Exam Point

Street children face a "triple burden" of health issues:

- **Infectious Diseases (HIV/AIDS):** They are particularly vulnerable to HIV/AIDS because they are less likely to have information about the disease or access to condoms and testing. They often face repeated risks of infection through exploitation or drug use.
- **Substance Abuse:** Exposure to "drug culture" is high. Many street children use volatile solvents (glue, petrol, thinners) or tobacco as an "==escape phenomenon==" from the tensions and frustrations of street life.
- **Mental Health Issues:** They often suffer from emotional instability, feelings of insecurity, and "failure to adjust" to the environment, which can lead to psychopathic behavior or permanent intellectual impairment.
- **Juvenile Delinquency:** A child on the street who commits an offense (theft, burglary, etc.) is labeled a juvenile delinquent. This is viewed as a **social problem** requiring rehabilitation rather than just punishment.

---

### **IV. Preventive and Social Defence Measures**

India utilizes a **Social Defence** approach to protect and rehabilitate street children.

1. **The Children Act, 1960:** This is the primary legislation providing for the care, protection, maintenance, and training of neglected or delinquent children.
2. **Institutional Infrastructure:**
    - **Remand Homes:** For temporary stay during the investigation of their status.
    - **Certified Schools:** For long-term vocational training and education.
    - **After-care Facilities:** To help them reintegrate into society and prevent a "relapse" into street life.
3. **National Institute of Social Defence (1975):** Coordinates the correctional and rehabilitative services at a national level.
4. **Community-Based Interventions:** Programs like **ASHA** and local NGOs are used to identify at-risk children and link them to **Adolescent Friendly Health Clinics (SNEHA/AFHC)**.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points** ★

- **Social Pathology:** The term used to link disease (like HIV or drug use) to social conditions (like living on the street).
- **Vulnerability to HIV:** Street children are a key population because they lack information and face repeated risks.
- **The Children Act, 1960:** The core legal framework for managing street and delinquent children in India.
- **Mental Deprivation:** A term coined by Dr. John Bowlby to describe the impact of a broken home on a child's development.

---

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: What is "Social Defence"?**
    - **A:** It is a system designed to defend society against criminality not just by punishing offenders, but by creating conditions (rehabilitation, education) conducive to healthy human growth.
- **Q: Why is HIV risk higher in street children?**
    - **A:** Due to a lack of health literacy, marginalization, and high exposure to exploitation and drug use.
- **Q: What is a "Remand Home"?**
    - **A:** A protective facility where children are kept temporarily while the court or welfare board decides on the best course for their rehabilitation.

---

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Punitive Tone:** Writing that street children should be "punished" for crimes. In Community Medicine, the focus is always on **rehabilitation** and **social defence**.
- **Narrow Scope:** Focusing only on poverty and forgetting the role of **broken homes** and **psychosocial stress** as primary drivers.
- **Missing the Act:** Forgetting to mention **The Children Act, 1960**, which is a high-scoring keyword in university exams.
