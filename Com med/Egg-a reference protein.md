[[NUTRITION]]
In Community Medicine and Nutrition, the hen’s egg is considered the gold standard for evaluating the quality of other dietary proteins. It is the primary "reference protein" used by international and national health agencies to assess nutritional adequacy.

---

### **I. Introduction and Definition**

- **Definition of Reference Protein:** A reference protein is a high-quality protein that contains all the essential amino acids in the correct proportions required by the human body.
- **The Hen’s Egg:** Because of its near-perfect amino acid profile and high digestibility, the protein in a hen's egg is designated as the "reference protein" against which all other food proteins (like pulses, cereals, or meat) are compared.

---

### **II. Nutritive Value of the Egg** ★ Important Exam Point

The egg is a powerhouse of nutrients, providing high-quality macronutrients and essential vitamins.

- **Protein Content:** A single hen’s egg weighing approximately **60 g** contains **6 g of protein**.
- **Type of Protein:** The primary protein found in the egg white is **ovalbumin**.
- **Nutrient Profile:** Egg contains almost all essential nutrients required by the body, with two notable exceptions: **carbohydrates** and **Vitamin C**.

---

### **III. Why is it the "Reference"? (Pathogenesis of Quality)**

The "reference" status of the egg is based on two key biological indicators:

1. **Complete Protein:** It contains all the essential amino acids in the right proportions for human growth and maintenance.
2. **Net Protein Utilization (NPU):**
    - The NPU of egg protein is **100**.
    - This means 100% of the nitrogen from the egg protein is retained and used by the body for tissue building and repair.
    - **Comparison:** By contrast, the NPU of proteins in typical Indian diets (which are largely cereal and pulse-based) is only about **50 to 80**.
3. **Biological Value & Digestibility:** It has the highest possible biological value and high digestibility coefficient among all natural foods.

---

### **IV. Applications: Assessment of Protein Quality** ★ Important Exam Point

In laboratory and community nutrition surveys, egg protein is used as the standard for two main calculations:

#### **1. Amino Acid (Chemical) Score**

This is a measure of the concentration of each essential amino acid in a "test protein" (e.g., wheat) expressed as a percentage of that same amino acid in the "reference protein" (egg).

- **Formula:** $$\text{Amino acid score} = \frac{\text{mg of amino acid in 1g of test protein}}{\text{mg of same amino acid in 1g of reference protein}} \times 100$$
- **Scoring:** Animal foods typically score 70–80, while starches/cereals score 50–60.

#### **2. Net Protein Utilization (NPU)**

NPU is a more complete expression of protein quality because it accounts for both digestibility and how well the amino acids match the body's needs.

- **Formula:** $$\text{NPU} = \frac{\text{Nitrogen retained by the body}}{\text{Nitrogen intake}} \times 100$$

---

### **V. Social and Public Health Considerations**

While nutritionally superior, the use of eggs in India is influenced by cultural and safety factors:

- **Food Safety (Salmonellosis):** Eggs are a primary vehicle for **Salmonella** food poisoning. The bacteria can even penetrate the ovaries of the hen, contaminating the egg before the shell is formed.
- **Cultural Beliefs:** In the "hot and cold" food theory prevalent in India, eggs are considered to generate "heat" in the body. This leads some communities to avoid them during pregnancy or for young children with "delicate stomachs".
- **Religious Taboos:** Orthodox Hindus and Jains may avoid eggs entirely on religious grounds, relying instead on pulses for protein.

### **High-Yield Exam Points** ★

- **NPU Value:** Always remember the NPU of an egg is **100**.
- **Protein Amount:** 1 egg = 6g protein.
- **Limiting Nutrients:** Eggs lack **Carbohydrates** and **Vitamin C**.
- **Standard for Comparison:** Used to calculate the **Chemical Score** of other foods.

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: Why is egg protein called a 'Reference Protein'?**
    - **A:** Because it contains all essential amino acids in optimal proportions and has the maximum biological value/NPU (100).
- **Q: What is the main protein in egg white?**
    - **A:** Ovalbumin.
- **Q: Which nutrients are absent in an egg?**
    - **A:** Carbohydrates and Vitamin C.
- **Q: What is the risk associated with raw or undercooked eggs?**
    - **A:** Salmonellosis (Food poisoning).

---

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Confusing NPU values:** Students often forget that egg is the only food with an NPU of 100.
- **Ignoring the shell risk:** Failing to mention that Salmonella can enter through shell cracks or be present inside the egg from the hen’s ovaries.
- **Mixing up "Reference Protein" and "Reference Woman":** The "Reference Woman" is a demographic concept (18-29 years, 55kg), whereas "Reference Protein" is a nutritional quality standard.
