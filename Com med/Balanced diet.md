[[NUTRITION]]
A **balanced diet** is a cornerstone of health and disease prevention. In Community Medicine, it is defined not merely as "eating enough," but as the consumption of a variety of foods in such quantities and proportions that the body's requirements for energy, proteins, vitamins, minerals, and other nutrients are adequately met, with a small provision for periods of leanness.

---

### **I. Definition and Introduction**

- **Definition:** A balanced diet provides all essential nutrients in the correct proportions to maintain health, promote growth, and protect against disease.
- **The "Triple Burden":** Failure to maintain a balanced diet leads to malnutrition, which includes undernutrition (deficiencies), micronutrient deficiencies (hidden hunger), and overnutrition (obesity).
- **Standard Basis:** In India, current recommendations are often based on the **"My Plate for the Day"** concept for a **2000 Kcal** intake, as formulated by ICMR-NIN Hyderabad.

---

### **II. Components of a Balanced Diet** ★ Important Exam Point

The components of a diet are classified as "Nutrient Agents." These are essential for the physiological and metabolic functions of the human body.

|**Component**|**Description and Role**|
|:--|:--|
|**1. Carbohydrates**|The primary source of energy. Recommended to focus on **complex carbohydrates** (whole grains, legumes, vegetables) rather than refined sugars.|
|**2. Proteins**|Essential for tissue building, repair, and immune function. Requirements vary by age, sex, and physiological state.|
|**3. Fats**|A concentrated energy source and carrier of fat-soluble vitamins. Includes saturated, monounsaturated (MUFA), and polyunsaturated (PUFA) fatty acids.|
|**4. Vitamins**|Organic compounds required in small amounts (e.g., Vitamin A for vision, Vitamin C for immunity, Folic Acid for blood formation).|
|**5. Minerals**|Inorganic elements like **Iron** (to prevent anaemia), **Iodine** (for thyroid and brain function), and **Calcium**(for bone health).|
|**6. Water**|A vital nutrient necessary for all cellular processes and temperature regulation.|
|**7. Dietary Fibre**|Non-starch polysaccharides that aid digestion and reduce the risk of diabetes and heart disease.|

### **III. Recommended Dietary Goals** ★ Important Exam Point

Dietary goals are formulated to prevent chronic Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs) like Hypertension, Diabetes, and Coronary Heart Disease (CHD).

#### **1. Macronutrient Distribution**

- **Total Fat Intake:** Should be restricted to **20–30%** of total energy intake.
- **Saturated Fats:** Must be limited to **less than 10%** of total energy intake.
- **Dietary Cholesterol:** Should be below **100 mg per 1000 Kcal** per day.
- **Free Sugars:** Should be reduced to **less than 10%** (ideally < 5%) of total energy intake.
- Protein- 10-15% of daily intake
- Sources rich in fat and alcohol should be avoided.
- Junk foods - 0 calories should be reduced

#### **2. Micronutrients and Fibre**

- **Salt (Sodium):** Intake should be reduced to **less than 5 g daily** to prevent hypertension.
- **Dietary Fibre:** A minimum daily intake of **20 grams** is recommended.
- **Potassium:** High intake of potassium-rich foods (fruits/vegetables) is encouraged as it antagonizes the ill effects of sodium on blood pressure.

#### **3. Recent Updates (2024 Guidelines)**

- **HFSS Foods:** Current policies strongly recommend avoiding foods **High in Fat, Sugar, and Salt** (HFSS) and **Ultra-Processed Foods** to curb the obesity and diabetes epidemic.
- **Total Energy Balance:** The energy intake should not exceed the energy expenditure required for one's physical activity level.

---

### **IV. Public Health Significance**

- **The First 1,000 Days:** A balanced diet from conception to age two is critical to prevent irreversible physical stunting and mental impairment.
- **Hidden Hunger:** Even if a person consumes enough calories, a lack of micronutrients (Iodine, Zinc, Iron) can lead to reduced productivity and increased mortality.
- **Lifecycle Influence:** A balanced diet reduces risks at every stage, from low birth weight in infants to osteoporosis and dementia in the elderly.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points** ★

- **Reference Calorie:** 2000 Kcal is the current standard used for the "My Plate" concept in India.
- **The "5g Rule":** Always mention that daily salt intake should not exceed 5 grams.
- **NPU of Egg:** In discussions of protein quality, remember that the **Net Protein Utilization (NPU)** of a hen's egg is **100**, making it the "reference protein".
- **HFSS:** This abbreviation (High Fat, Sugar, and Salt) is a mandatory keyword in current nutrition answers.

---

### **Frequently Asked Viva Questions**

- **Q: What is "Hidden Hunger"?**
    - **A:** It is a form of undernutrition where the intake of vitamins and minerals is too low, even if the person consumes enough calories (macronutrients).
- **Q: Why is salt intake restricted to 5g?**
    - **A:** High salt intake is a major modifiable risk factor for hypertension and cardiovascular diseases.
- **Q: What is the difference between simple and complex carbohydrates?**
    - **A:** Simple carbohydrates (sugars) cause rapid glucose spikes; complex carbohydrates (whole grains, legumes) provide sustained energy and essential fibre.

---

### **Common Mistakes Students Make**

- **Focusing only on Calories:** Students often define a balanced diet solely in terms of energy. You must include **micronutrients** and **fibre** to get full marks.
- **Missing Recent Updates:** Failing to mention the **2024 HFSS guidelines** or the **"My Plate"** concept makes the answer appear outdated.
- **Confusing Saturated and Unsaturated Fats:** Ensure you specify that **Saturated fats** should be limited (<10%), while **Unsaturated fats** (oils like soybean or sunflower) are healthier alternatives.
