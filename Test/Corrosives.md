**Carbolic Acid (Phenol) Poisoning**

---

**1. Basic Concepts**

**Introduction** **Carbolic Acid (Phenol)** is a potent **organic corrosive** poison derived from coal tar. It is commonly used as a household antiseptic and disinfectant. Common household products containing phenol derivatives include **Lysol** (50% cresol in saponified vegetable oil) and **Dettol** (chlorinated phenol with turpineol).

**Definition** A corrosive is a xenobiotic that causes functional and histological damage immediately upon contact with tissue surfaces. Carbolic acid is unique because it also has significant systemic toxic effects after absorption.

**Toxicokinetics & Metabolism**

- **Absorption:** It is rapidly absorbed through the skin, lungs (inhalation), and mucous membranes (ingestion, rectum, or vagina).
- **Metabolism:** Phenol is metabolized in the liver into **hydroquinone** and **pyrocatechol**.
- **Excretion:** It is excreted in the urine, partly in an unstable combination with glucuronic acid. Further oxidation of these metabolites in the urine causes a dark smoky green coloration known as **Carboluria**.

---

**2. Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Suicide:** It is frequently used for suicidal purposes due to easy availability.
- **Accidental:** Common in industrial settings or through accidental household ingestion.
- **Homicide:** Rare, because of its characteristic pungent odor and bitter taste, making it difficult to disguise in food.
- **Abortifacient:** Occasionally used as a local application to procure criminal abortion.
- **Occupational Hazard:** Chronic exposure can lead to **"Carbol Marasmus"** (chronic poisoning).

★ **Important Exam Point: Privileged Communication** A doctor’s duty to report a poisoning case to the state under **33 BNSS** overrides the duty of professional secrecy to the patient.

---

**3. Relevant Law (BNS, BNSS, BSA, 2023)**

---

**4. Mechanism of Action**

1. **Local Action:** It acts as a corrosive by producing **coagulative necrosis**, precipitating tissue proteins into a grayish-white slough. Unlike mineral acids, it also acts as a **local anesthetic**, meaning the initial burning sensation is followed by numbness.
2. **Systemic Action:** Being fat-soluble, it rapidly attacks the **Central Nervous System (CNS)**, causing paralysis of the respiratory and cardiovascular centers.

---

**5. Clinical Features**

Acute poisoning by carbolic acid is known as **Carbolism**.

- **Gastrointestinal (GIT):** Intense burning pain from mouth to stomach, followed by numbness (anesthesia). Vomiting is rare.
- **Urine (Carboluria):** Urine appears normal when passed but turns **smoky green** or brownish-black upon standing and exposure to air.
- **Ocular:** Pupils are characteristically **constricted (miosis)** and non-reacting.
- **Odor:** A characteristic "Phenolic" or spirit-like odor is present in the breath.
- **Vital Signs:** Pulse is rapid and feeble; skin is cold, clammy, and sweating.
- **Chronic Poisoning (Carbol Marasmus):** Characterized by anorexia, weight loss, headache, and **Ochronosis** (bluish-black discoloration of cartilages and cornea).

---

**6. Management**

★ **Important Exam Point: Gastric Lavage Exception** In most corrosive poisonings, gastric lavage is contraindicated due to the risk of perforation. However, **phenol is an exception** because it makes the stomach wall **leathery and tough**, making lavage relatively safe.

1. **Gastric Lavage:** Perform carefully using **lukewarm water**.
    - **Additives:** Activated charcoal (adsorbs poison), olive oil (demulcent), or sodium/magnesium sulfate (forms less toxic compounds).
2. **Demulcents:** Give milk, egg white, or starch water to protect the mucosa.
3. **Specific Antidote:** There is no specific chemical antidote.
4. **Skin Decontamination:** If phenol falls on the body, wash the area with soap and water; then apply **olive oil or methylated spirit** to prevent further absorption.
5. **Supportive Care:** IV fluids for shock, sodium bicarbonate for acidosis, and respiratory support (O2/Intubation).

---

**7. Medico-Legal Autopsy Findings**

- **External Findings:** Grayish or brownish corrosion at the angles of the mouth and chin. A characteristic phenolic odor is present.
- **Internal Findings:**
    - **Stomach:** The wall appears **hardened and leathery**. The mucosa is swollen and shows grayish or brownish slimy mucus.
    - **Lungs:** Laryngeal and pulmonary edema are common.
    - **Kidneys:** Signs of hemorrhagic nephritis.
- ★ **Note:** **Putrefaction is retarded** in phenol poisoning due to the preservative action of the acid on tissues.

---

**8. Specimen Collection & Preservation**

★ **Important Exam Point: PAPPA Mnemonic** Rectified spirit (95% Ethanol) is the ideal preservative for most viscera, but it is **contraindicated** in the **PAPPA** mnemonic: **P**henol, **A**lcohol, **P**hosphorus, **P**araldehyde, and **A**cetic acid.

|**Specimen**|**Quantity**|**Container**|**Preservative**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Stomach & Contents**|Entire|1L Glass jar|**Saturated Common Salt**.|
|**Liver/Kidneys**|500g / Half|1L Glass jar|Saturated Common Salt.|
|**Blood**|10 mL|Fluoride vial|Sodium Fluoride.|
|**Urine**|100 mL|Glass jar|Toluene or Thymol.|

---

**9. High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Leathery Stomach:** Diagnostic feature of phenol ingestion.
- **Carboluria:** Smoky green urine due to hydroquinone oxidation.
- **Miosis:** Constricted pupils, similar to opiate or organophosphate poisoning.
- **Ferric Chloride Test:** Adding 10% ferric chloride to urine produces a **purple or blue color** which persists even on heating (diagnostic for phenol).
- **Ochronosis:** Pigmentation of cartilages seen in chronic poisoning.

---

**10. Viva Questions & Model Answers**

1. **Why is gastric lavage performed in phenol poisoning despite it being a corrosive?**
    - _Ans:_ Because phenol causes coagulative necrosis that hardens the stomach mucosa, making it "leathery" and less prone to perforation than other mineral acids.
2. **What is the color of the stomach mucosa in nitric acid vs. carbolic acid?**
    - _Ans:_ Nitric acid causes yellow staining (Xanthoproteic reaction), while carbolic acid causes a grayish-white (buff-white) leathery appearance.
3. **What preservative is used for viscera in carbolic acid cases?**
    - _Ans:_ Saturated solution of common salt. Rectified spirit is avoided because it belongs to the PAPPA group of contraindications.

---

**Quick Revision Summary**

1. **Identity:** Organic corrosive; household antiseptic (Lysol/Dettol).
2. **Mechanism:** Coagulative necrosis + CNS depression.
3. **Signs:** **Leathery stomach**, **Constricted pupils**, **Smoky green urine** (Carboluria), Phenolic odor.
4. **Lavage:** Allowed (Exception).
5. **Law:** Section 33 BNSS (Inform police).
6. **Preservation:** Use Saturated Common Salt; avoid spirit.

**Oxalic Acid Poisoning**

---
[[Corrosives]]
**Basic Concepts**

**Definition** **Oxalic Acid (**H2​C2​O4​**)**, commonly known as **"Acid of Sugar,"** is a potent **organic corrosive** poison. A corrosive is a substance that causes functional and histological damage immediately upon contact with tissue surfaces.

**Introduction**

- **Physical Properties:** It occurs as colorless, transparent, prismatic crystals that closely resemble Magnesium Sulphate (MgSO4​) and Zinc Sulphate (ZnSO4​), which often leads to accidental ingestion.
- **Sources:** Naturally found in relatively high levels in dark-green leafy vegetables like spinach, rhubarb, beet leaves, and parsley.
- **Industrial/Household Uses:** Used for removing rust and blood stains from clothes, cleaning brass and car radiators, bleaching, and in the tanning industry. It is also used in forgery to remove signatures or writing from documents.

**Mechanism of Action**

1. **Local Action:** Acts as a corrosive on the skin and mucous membranes, though it is less severe than mineral acids like sulphuric acid.
2. **Remote/Systemic Action:**
    - **Hypocalcemia:** It extracts calcium from the tissues and blood to form insoluble **calcium oxalate**. This depletion of ionized calcium leads to tetany and cardiac disturbances.
    - **Renal Action:** Calcium oxalate crystals are deposited in the renal tubules, causing **acute tubular necrosis**, uremia, and renal failure.
    - **CVS:** Causes profound shock and cardiac arrest.

**Fatal Dose and Period**

- **Fatal Dose:** **15–20 grams**.
- **Fatal Period:** **1–2 hours**. If the patient survives the initial stage but develops renal failure, death may occur in 2 days to 2 weeks.

---

**Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Accidental Poisoning:** Most common; occurs due to its resemblance to Epsom salts (MgSO4​) or sodium bicarbonate.
- **Suicide/Homicide:** Rare, because of its very sour and bitter taste.
- **Abortifacient:** Occasionally used to induce criminal abortion.
- **Forgery:** Used to remove ink signatures.

---

**Relevant Law (BNS, BNSS, BSA, 2023)**

★ **Important Exam Point: Mandatory Reporting** Under the new criminal laws, doctors have strict obligations in poisoning cases.

|**Act**|**Section**|**Provision Name**|**Medico-Legal Relevance**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**BNSS**|**33**|Information to Police|**Mandatory** for doctors to inform the police/Magistrate in all poisoning cases.|
|**BNS**|**123**|Hurt by Poison|Administering oxalic acid with intent to cause hurt or facilitate a crime.|
|**BNS**|**211**|Non-compliance|Punishment if the doctor fails to report a mandatory case to authorities.|
|**BNS**|**286**|Negligence|Punishment for rash or negligent conduct regarding poisonous substances.|
|**BSA**|**26**|Dying Declaration|A statement made by the person about the cause of their impending death is admissible.|

---

**Clinical Features**

Poisoning manifests in three distinct forms depending on the dose and survival time:

**1. Fulminating Poisoning (Large dose >15g)**

- **Symptoms:** Occur within minutes. Burning, sour, or bitter taste in the mouth.
- **Gastrointestinal:** Intense burning pain from mouth to stomach, radiating over the abdomen.
- **Vomiting:** Frequent vomiting of **"coffee-ground"** appearance (dark brown/black due to altered blood).
- **Outcome:** Severe thirst, diarrhea, shock, and rapid death.

**2. Acute Poisoning (Intermediate Doses)**

- Symptoms are primarily due to **hypocalcemia** (low calcium):
    - Tingling and numbness of fingers and toes.
    - **Tetany:** Painful muscle contractions and carpopedal spasms.
    - ★ **Trousseau Sign:** Positive (spasm of the hand when a BP cuff is inflated).
    - Convulsions and dilated pupils.

**3. Delayed Poisoning (Small doses/Survival >24h)**

- Characterized by **nephritis** and renal failure:
    - Scanty urine (oliguria), albuminuria, and hematuria.
    - ★ **Oxaluria:** Presence of **envelope-shaped** calcium oxalate crystals in the urine (diagnostic).

---

**Management**

★ **Important Exam Point: Gastric Lavage Exception** In most corrosive poisonings, gastric lavage is contraindicated. However, in **Oxalic Acid** and **Carbolic Acid** poisoning, it **can be performed** with caution.

1. **Gastric Lavage:** Perform immediately using **Calcium Lactate** or lime water (Calcium Hydroxide).
    - _Mechanism:_ It precipitates the acid into insoluble and non-absorbable calcium oxalate.
2. **Antidotes:**
    - **Specific Antidote:** **Calcium Gluconate** (10 mL of 10% solution IV) given frequently to restore blood calcium levels.
    - **Calcium Lactate/Chloride:** Given orally.
3. **Demulcents:** Give plenty of milk or egg white to coat the mucosa.
4. **Avoid Alkalis:** Do **not** use sodium, potassium, or ammonium carbonates, as they form _soluble_ oxalates which are easily absorbed and highly toxic.
5. **Supportive Care:** IV fluids for shock, parathyroid extracts, and hemodialysis for renal failure.

---

**Medico-Legal Autopsy Findings**

**External Findings**

- Usually non-specific. Occasional burns/corrosion on the face and skin.

**Internal Findings**

- **Mucosa:** Mouth, tongue, and esophagus appear **bleached and corroded** (white/greyish).
- **Stomach:**
    - Mucosa is reddened and punctate with a **velvety red or blackish** appearance.
    - Contains gelatinous brown material.
    - ★ **Magenstrasse Effect:** Damage is often maximum along the **lesser curvature**.
- **Kidneys:**
    - Swollen and congested.
    - Histopathology shows **calcium oxalate crystals** in the tubules and necrosis of the proximal convoluted tubules (PCT).

---

**High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Envelope Crystals:** Calcium oxalate crystals in urine or kidney tubules are diagnostic.
- **Magenstrasse Effect:** Liquid acids flow along the lesser curvature; therefore, the stomach must be opened along the **greater curvature** during autopsy to preserve the damage.
- **PAPPA Contraindication:** Rectified spirit is the best preservative for acids, but it is contraindicated for **P**henol, **A**lcohol, **P**hosphorus, **P**araldehyde, and **A**cetic acid. For Oxalic acid, spirit is the correct choice.
- **Coffee-ground Vomitus:** Classic feature along with intense hypocalcemia.

---

**Viva Questions**

1. **What is the "Acid of Sugar"?**
    - _Ans:_ Oxalic acid, named so because it was originally prepared from sugar.
2. **Why are sodium carbonates avoided in treatment?**
    - _Ans:_ They react with oxalic acid to form _soluble_ oxalates which are rapidly absorbed into the blood, worsening systemic toxicity.
3. **What is the Trousseau sign?**
    - _Ans:_ A sign of latent tetany (hypocalcemia) where carpal spasm occurs after inflating a BP cuff on the arm.
4. **Which organ is the primary site of systemic toxicity besides the heart?**
    - _Ans:_ The Kidneys, due to the deposition of oxalate crystals in the tubules.

---

**Quick Revision Summary**

1. **Nature:** Organic corrosive; looks like Epsom salt.
2. **Mechanism:** Corrosive (local) + Hypocalcemia + Renal failure (systemic).
3. **Key Signs:** Coffee-ground vomitus, Tetany, Trousseau sign, Envelope crystals in urine.
4. **Treatment:** Calcium Lactate lavage + Calcium Gluconate IV.
5. **Preservation:** Use **Rectified Spirit** for viscera.
6. **Law:** Section 123 BNS and Section 33 BNSS.
