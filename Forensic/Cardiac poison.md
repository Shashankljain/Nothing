[[Toxicology]]
### **Cardiac Poisons**
#new 
---

#### **Basic Concepts**

**Definition** **Cardiac poisons** are substances (primarily plant-derived) that act specifically on the heart, either by affecting the heart musculature directly or through its nerve supply.

**Introduction** These poisons interfere with the heart's rhythm and electrical conduction. Because their symptoms (like arrhythmias) often resemble natural heart disease, they are frequently used in homicidal cases to escape detection.

**Classification of Cardiac Poisons** The following are the major plants and compounds classified as cardiac poisons:

|**Name of Poison**|**Scientific Name**|**Key Active Principles**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Aconite**|_Aconitum napellus / ferox_|**Aconitine**, Pseudoaconitine, Mesaconitine|
|**Digitalis**|_Digitalis purpurea_|**Digitoxin**, Digoxin, Digitalin|
|**White Oleander**|_Nerium odorum_|**Nerin**, Neriodorein, Karabin|
|**Yellow Oleander**|_Cascabela thevetia_|**Thevetin**, Thevotoxin, Cerberin|
|**Odollam**|_Cerbera odollam_|**Cerberin**, Neriifolin|
|**Tobacco**|_Nicotiana tabacum_|**Nicotine**, Anabasine, Lobeline|
|**Quinine**|_Cinchona species_|Quinine alkaloids|

### **Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Ideal Homicidal Poison (★ Important Exam Point):** **Aconite** is often regarded as an ideal homicidal poison because it is cheap, easily available, requires a small fatal dose, and its taste can be masked by sweets or betel leaves. Furthermore, it is extremely unstable and destroyed by putrefaction, making chemical detection difficult.
- **Suicide:** **Yellow Oleander** and **Odollam** (Suicide tree) are common suicidal agents, particularly in Kerala.
- **Accidental:** Consumption of aconite root by mistaking it for horseradish or use of quack remedies.
- **Malingering:** Tobacco leaves are sometimes soaked in water and placed in the axilla to induce poisonous symptoms (tachycardia) for the purpose of feigning illness.
- **Cattle Poison:** Aconite, Digitalis, and Oleander are used for poisoning cattle.

### **Topic-Specific Explanation: Major Cardiac Poisons**

#### **1. Aconite (Monk's Hood / Mitha Zaher)**

- **Mechanism:** Acts on voltage-sensitive sodium channels, first stimulating and then paralyzing sensory and secretory nerves, the CNS, and the myocardium.
- **Clinical Features:**
    - **Tingling and Numbness:** Characteristic "bitter-sweet" taste followed by intense tingling and numbness of the tongue, mouth, and skin.
    - **Hippus (★ Important Exam Point):** Pupils alternately contract and dilate (also called pupillary athetosis).
    - **Cardiovascular:** Hypotension, chest pain, bradycardia, and ventricular arrhythmias.
- **Fatal Dose:** 1–2 g of root; 2–5 mg of aconitine.
- **Fatal Period:** 2–6 hours.

#### **2. Digitalis (Foxglove)**

- **Mechanism:** Inhibits the Na+/K+ ATPase enzyme in cardiac myocytes, increasing intracellular calcium and prolonging the diastolic period.
- **Clinical Features:**
    - **Ocular:** Blurring of vision and characteristic **yellow-green vision** (Xanthopsia).
    - **CVS:** Bradycardia, heart block, and extrasystoles.
- **Fatal Dose:** 2–3 g (leaves); 5 mg (digoxin).

#### **3. Oleander (White and Yellow)**

- **White Oleander (_Nerium odorum_):** Acts like Digitalis on the heart but also acts on the spinal cord like **Strychnine**, causing tetanic spasms and lockjaw.
- **Yellow Oleander (_Cascabela thevetia_):** Extremely powerful cardiac poison; clinical effects are similar to Digitalis, often leading to heart block and peripheral circulatory failure.

### **Management of Cardiac Poisoning**

1. **Immediate Management (ABCD):** Stabilize airway and circulation.
2. **Gastric Lavage:** Performed with **Tannic acid** or **Activated charcoal** to precipitate or adsorb the plant alkaloids.
3. **Specific Antidotes:**
    - **Digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digoxin-Fab):** Specifically used to treat life-threatening arrhythmias in Digitalis or Odollam poisoning.
    - **Atropine (0.5–1 mg IV):** Used to treat severe bradycardia.
4. **Anti-arrhythmic Drugs:** Lignocaine, amiodarone, or flecainide are used to manage ventricular tachyarrhythmias.

---

### **Autopsy Findings**

- **General:** Signs of asphyxia (cyanosis, congestion) are often present.
- **Stomach:** May contain fragments of the specific leaves or roots.
- **Heart (Specific Finding):**
    - **Aconite:** Heart shows diffuse **contraction-band necrosis** in the myocardium.
    - **Oleander:** Characteristic **petechial hemorrhages** on the heart.
- **Organs:** Generally congested.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Hippus:** Diagnostic sign for Aconite poisoning (pupils dancing).
- ★ **Ideal Homicidal Poison:** Aconite (due to low dose and resemblance to heart disease).
- ★ **Yellow-Green Vision:** Hallmark of Digitalis toxicity.
- ★ **Malingering:** Use of tobacco in the axilla to simulate cardiac symptoms.
- ★ **Migenstrasse Effect:** Corrosives follow the lesser curvature of the stomach, but cardiac plant fragments are found throughout the lumen.

### **Viva Questions**

1. **Why is Aconite called 'Wolf's bane'?**
    - _Ans:_ Historically, it was used to poison meat laid out to kill wolves.
2. **What is the specific antidote for Digitalis?**
    - _Ans:_ Digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digoxin-Fab).
3. **How do you differentiate Aconite root from Horseradish?**
    - _Ans:_ Aconite root is conical, shriveled, and dark brown, while Horseradish is long, cylindrical, and pale.
4. **Which cardiac poison is used as a 'truth serum'?**
    - _Ans:_ **Scopolamine** (an alkaloid found in Dhatura/Belladonna, not primarily a cardiac poison but often mentioned alongside them).

### **Quick Revision Summary**

1. **Nature:** Systemic poisons acting on the heart musculature/nerves.
2. **Key Agents:** Aconite (Sodium channels), Digitalis (Na+/K+ ATPase), Oleander (Tetanic spasms + Heart block).
3. **Signs:** Tingling, numbness, arrhythmias, hippus, and visual changes.
4. **Treatment:** Lavage (Tannic acid) + Atropine + Digoxin-Fab.
5. **Autopsy:** Contraction-band necrosis (Aconite) and subendocardial petechiae.
6. **Law:** Section 33 BNSS (2023) makes reporting mandatory.
