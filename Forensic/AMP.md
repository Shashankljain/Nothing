### **Toxicology and Acute Methyl Alcohol Poisoning**

---

#### **Basic Concepts**

**Definition of Toxicology** **Toxicology** is the science dealing with the properties, actions, toxicity, fatal dose, detection, estimation, and treatment of poisonous substances, as well as the autopsy findings in cases of death.

- **Forensic Toxicology:** Deals with the medical and legal aspects of the harmful effects of chemicals on humans, involving the identification and interpretation of poisons in tissues.
- **Clinical Toxicology:** Focuses on human diseases caused by or associated with abnormal exposure to chemical substances.

**Introduction to Methyl Alcohol** **Methyl Alcohol (Methanol)**, also known as **Wood Alcohol, Wood Naphtha, or Wood Spirits**, is a colorless, volatile liquid with a characteristic odor similar to ethyl alcohol and a burning taste. It is widely used in industries as a solvent and is a common constituent of cleaning materials, antifreeze, and duplicating fluids.

---

### **Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Accidental Poisoning (Hooch Tragedies):** The most common form, occurring due to the consumption of adulterated liquor where methanol is used as a cheap substitute for ethanol.
- **Suicide and Homicide:** Occur but are not common.
- **Stupefying Agent:** Sometimes added to illicit liquor to increase its perceived potency.

★ **Important Exam Point: Legal Reporting** Under the new criminal laws of 2023, doctors have a mandatory duty to report poisoning cases.

|**Act**|**Section**|**Provision**|**Medico-Legal Relevance**|
|:--|:--|:--|:--|
|**BNSS**|**33**|Information to Police|**Mandatory** for doctors to inform authorities in all poisoning cases.|
|**BNS**|**123**|Hurt by Poison|Administering poison with intent to cause hurt or facilitate a crime.|

### **Toxicological Aspects of Acute Methanol Poisoning**

#### **1. Toxicokinetics (Metabolism)**

- **Absorption:** Rapidly absorbed from the stomach, intestines, lungs, and skin.
- **Metabolism:** Methanol is a cumulative poison because its oxidation is slow (15% the rate of ethyl alcohol).
- **Path:** **Methanol $\rightarrow$ Formaldehyde $\rightarrow$ Formic Acid** (metabolized by alcohol dehydrogenase).
- **Toxic Metabolite:** **Formic acid** is the primary culprit, causing a high anion-gap metabolic acidosis and specific retinal toxicity.

#### **2. Toxicodynamics (Mechanism of Action)**

- It causes CNS depression similar to ethanol but with much higher toxicity.
- The mechanism of cellular toxicity is similar to **cyanide** or carbon monoxide poisoning, as it inhibits oxidative processes in the tissues.

#### **3. Fatal Dose and Period**

- **Fatal Dose:** **60–140 mL** (blood levels >150 mg/dL are usually fatal).
- **Fatal Period:** **24–36 hours**.

#### **4. Clinical Features**

Symptoms usually occur after a latent period of **12–24 hours**.

- **Gastrointestinal:** Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and a spirit-like odor in the breath.
- **Ocular (Classical Sign):**
    - ★ **"Snowfield Vision"**: Blurred or misty vision, as if in a snowstorm.
    - 'Halo vision' (bright circles around lights), fixed and dilated pupils, and eventual permanent blindness due to optic nerve atrophy.
- **CNS:** Headache, dizziness, delirium, and coma.
- **Renal:** Severe metabolic acidosis and scanty urine.

---

### **Management**

1. **Gastric Lavage:** Performed with 5% sodium bicarbonate solution to prevent absorption.
2. **Combat Acidosis:** Administration of IV Sodium Bicarbonate (2 g in 250 mL of water) is critical to correct the metabolic acidosis.
3. **Specific Antidotes:**
    - **Ethanol:** Administered as a competitive antagonist. It has a much higher affinity for alcohol dehydrogenase than methanol, thus preventing the formation of toxic formic acid. (Desired blood ethanol: 100–150 mg/dL).
    - **Fomepizole (4-methylpyrazole):** A potent competitive inhibitor of alcohol dehydrogenase; can be used instead of ethanol.
4. **Folate Therapy:** Calcium folinate (Leucovorin) helps enhance the metabolism of the toxic formate.
5. **Supportive Care:** Hemodialysis is used in severe cases to remove methanol and formic acid.

---

### **Autopsy Examination Findings**

If the victim dies, the following findings are typically observed during the medico-legal autopsy:

#### **External Findings**

- Signs of **asphyxia** with marked cyanosis.
- **Postmortem Staining:** Prominent and often **purple or blackish** in color.
- **Froth:** May be present at the mouth and nostrils.
- Skin may have a purple tinge if pyridine was present in the adulterated liquor.

#### **Internal Findings**

- **Stomach:** Mucosa of the stomach and duodenum is congested and inflamed, showing small **hemorrhages**.
- **Lungs:** Heavily congested and edematous.
- **Liver:** Shows signs of **necrobiosis** and fatty change.
- **Kidneys:** Acute **tubular degeneration**.
- **Brain:**
    - Diffuse cerebral edema.
    - ★ **Symmetrical Infarction:** Selectively affects the **putamen** and sub-cortical white matter.
- **Urinary Bladder:** Mucosa appears congested.
- **Viscera:** All internal organs are generally congested and may smell of alcohol.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Snowfield Vision:** The hallmark visual disturbance of methanol poisoning.
- ★ **Formic Acid:** The toxic metabolite responsible for acidosis and blindness.
- ★ **Putamen Necrosis:** A specific brain finding at autopsy.
- ★ **Antidote Triad:** Ethanol, Fomepizole, and Folate.
- ★ **Preservation:** Viscera should be preserved in **Saturated Salt** (spirit is avoided in alcohol poisoning cases).

---

### **Quick Revision Summary**

1. **Nature:** Industrial solvent/adulterant in "Hooch."
2. **Metabolism:** Methanol $\rightarrow$ Formaldehyde $\rightarrow$ Formic Acid.
3. **Signs:** High anion-gap acidosis, "Snowfield" vision, blindness.
4. **Treatment:** Sodium Bicarbonate + Ethanol/Fomepizole + Hemodialysis.
5. **Autopsy:** Putamen necrosis, cerebral edema, and purple/black PM staining.
6. **Law:** Section 33 BNSS (Reporting is mandatory for medical officers).
