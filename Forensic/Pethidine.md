### **Pethidine (Meperidine)**
[[Somniferous]]
---

#### **Basic Concepts**

**Definition**  
**Pethidine**, also known as **Meperidine**, is a synthetic opioid compound. In forensic toxicology, it is classified as a **Somniferous (Sleep-inducing) Narcotic Poison** and a **Cerebral Neurotic Poison**.

**Introduction**  
Unlike morphine or codeine, which are natural opiates derived directly from the poppy plant (_Papaver somniferum_), Pethidine is a **synthetic opioid**. It is chemically manufactured to produce opium-like effects by binding to specific receptors in the brain.

**Classification**

- **Type:** Synthetic Opioid.
- **Form:** Usually available as a hydrochloride salt.

**Mechanism of Action**

- **Receptor Binding:** Pethidine acts by binding to opioid receptors (**mu, kappa, and delta**) distributed throughout the nervous system.
- **Neurotransmission:** Activation of these receptors inhibits synaptic neurotransmission in both the Central Nervous System (CNS) and Peripheral Nervous System (PNS).
- **Chemical Behaviour:** It behaves chemically like an alkaloid (a nitrogenous base that unites with acids to form salts).

---

#### **Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Suicide:** Opioids are considered a "poison of choice" for suicide because they result in a relatively painless death.
- **Addiction:** It is a popular drug of abuse and is used for recreational purposes.
- **Date Rape:** Because it produces a state of relaxation and disinhibition, it can be used as a "date rape" drug when placed into drinks.
- **Medical Negligence:** Negligence may be alleged if a patient is discharged too soon after Naloxone treatment, as rebound toxicity can occur once the antidote wears off.

---

#### **Relevant Law (BNS & BNSS, 2023)**

Medical practitioners must handle cases of pethidine poisoning/abuse under the following provisions:

|**BNSS**|**33**|Information to Police|**Mandatory** for a doctor to inform the police in any poisoning case (privileged communication).|

#### **Clinical Features (Opiate Toxidrome)**

The clinical presentation of Pethidine poisoning follows the **Opiate Toxidrome**:

1. **Classic Triad:** **Respiratory Depression + Pin-point Pupils (Miosis) + Impairment of Sensorium (Coma)**.
2. **Stages of Poisoning:**
    - **Stage of Excitement:** Brief period of euphoria, laughter, and increased confidence.
    - **Stage of Stupor:** Headache, nausea, vomiting, and extreme drowsiness. The patient can be aroused by painful stimuli.
    - **Stage of Narcosis/Coma:** Deep coma, flaccid muscles, and non-reacting pupils. The skin becomes cold and clammy with profuse perspiration.
3. **Terminal Signs:** Development of **ARDS** (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome) and a **"foam cone"** (pinkish froth) at the mouth.

---

#### **Management**

★ **Important Exam Point: Management follows the ABCD protocol.**

**I. Immediate Management (ABCD)**

- **Airway:** Establish a patent airway via positioning or intubation.
- **Breathing:** Provide mechanical ventilation or supplemental oxygen.
- **Circulation:** Maintain blood pressure and tissue perfusion.

**II. Gastric Decontamination**

- **Gastric Lavage:** Performed with **Potassium Permanganate (KMnO₄ 1:5000)**.
    - _Note:_ Lavage is performed even if the drug was taken parenterally (injection), as opioids are secreted back into the stomach juice.
- **Activated Charcoal:** Used to adsorb the poison and prevent further absorption.

**III. Specific Antidote**

- **Antidote:** **Naloxone** is the specific narcotic antagonist.
- **Dose:** Initial dose of **0.4–2 mg IV/IM**, repeated every 2–3 minutes up to a total of 10 mg.

---

#### **Autopsy Findings**

**External Findings**

- **Smell:** Faint smell of the drug may be perceived.
- **Cyanosis:** Face and body appear bluish or blackish.
- **PM Staining:** Prominent and purple or blackish in color.
- **Foam:** Froth at the mouth and nostrils.

**Internal Findings**

- **Brain:** Diffuse cerebral edema.
- **Lungs:** Heavily congested and edematous; trachea contains frothy secretions.
- **Stomach:** Mucosa is congested.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Antidote:** Naloxone (0.4–2 mg).
- ★ **Diagnostic Triad:** Coma, Miosis, and Respiratory Depression.
- ★ **Lavage Fluid:** 1:5000 Potassium Permanganate.
- ★ **Legal Requirement:** Reporting is mandatory under **Section 33 BNSS**.

### **Viva Questions**

1. **Is Pethidine a natural or synthetic opioid?**
    - _Ans:_ It is a synthetic opioid.
2. **Why do you perform gastric lavage in an injection case of pethidine?**
    - _Ans:_ Because the drug is secreted from the blood back into the stomach juice after absorption.
3. **What is the specific antagonist for Pethidine?**
    - _Ans:_ Naloxone.
4. **What does "Miosis" mean in the context of opioid poisoning?**
    - _Ans:_ It refers to pin-point, constricted pupils.
