[[Cardiac poison]]
### **Aconite Poisoning (Monk’s Hood)**

---

#### **1. Basic Concepts**

- **Definition:** Aconite is a highly potent **cardiac plant poison** derived from the species _Aconitum napellus_ or _Aconitum ferox_.
- **Common Names:** Monk’s hood, _Mitha zaher_, _Bish_, Wolf’s bane, Devil’s helmet, or Blue rocket.
- **Active Principles:** The most potent alkaloids are **aconitine**, pseudoaconitine, and mesaconitine.
- **Toxicokinetics (Mechanism):**
    - **Action:** It acts on **voltage-sensitive sodium channels**, first stimulating and then paralyzing the peripheral terminations of sensory and secretory nerves, the Central Nervous System (CNS), and the myocardium.
    - ★ **Important Exam Point:** It does **not** affect the higher centers of the brain; therefore, the victim remains **fully conscious** until the very end.

---

#### **2. Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Ideal Homicidal Poison:** Aconite is frequently tested as an "Ideal Homicidal Poison" because:
    1. It is cheap and easily available.
    2. The fatal dose is very small, and the fatal period is short.
    3. The taste can be masked by sweets or _betel (paan)_ leaves.
    4. It is extremely unstable and destroyed by putrefaction, making it difficult to detect during chemical analysis.
- **Accidental:** Often occurs when the root is mistaken for **Horseradish** or through quack remedies.
- **Abortifacient:** Sometimes used to procure criminal abortion.
- **Cattle Poison:** Used in rural areas to kill livestock.

---

#### **3. Relevant Law (BNS & BNSS, 2023)**

The following provisions apply under the new Indian criminal codes:

- **Section 33 BNSS:** **Mandatory reporting** of any poisoning case to the police by the medical officer.
- **Section 123 BNS:** Administering poison with the **intent to cause hurt** or commit an offence (formerly 328 IPC).
- **Section 101 BNS:** Punishment for **Murder** if the poisoning results in death (formerly 302 IPC).
- **Section 286 BNS:** Punishment for **negligent conduct** with respect to poisonous substances.

---

#### **4. Clinical Features**

Symptoms typically appear within **15–30 minutes** of ingestion.

|**System**|**Signs and Symptoms**|
|:--|:--|
|**Gastrointestinal (GIT)**|**Bitter-sweet taste**, intense burning/tingling of the tongue and mouth followed by **numbness**; nausea and vomiting.|
|**Cardiovascular (CVS)**|Bradycardia (slow heart rate), palpitations, hypotension, and fatal **ventricular arrhythmias**.|
|**Ocular (★ Specific Sign)**|**Hippus:** The pupils alternately contract and dilate (pupillary athetosis). There is also blurred or yellow-green vision.|
|**CNS/Muscular**|Paresthesia (pins and needles) in limbs; weakness and spasms.|

- **Fatal Dose:** 1–2 g of root; 2–5 mg of pure aconitine.
- **Fatal Period:** **2–6 hours**.

---

#### **5. Management**

Management is primarily supportive as there is **no specific antidote**.

1. **Immediate Stabilization (ABCD):** Maintain airway and support respiration.
2. **Gastric Decontamination:** Gastric lavage with **Tannic acid** or **Activated charcoal** to precipitate the alkaloids.
3. **Cardiac Management:**
    - **Atropine (0.5–1 mg IV):** To treat severe bradycardia.
    - **Anti-arrhythmics:** Amiodarone or Flecainide for ventricular tachycardia.
4. **Supportive:** IV fluids for hypotension; oxygen for respiratory distress.

---

#### **6. Post-Mortem Findings**

- **External:** Non-specific signs of asphyxia (cyanosis, congestion).
- **Internal:**
    - **Stomach:** May contain fragments of the conical root.
    - **Heart (★ Key Finding):** Diffuse **contraction-band necrosis** in the myocardium.
    - **Lungs:** Hemorrhagic pulmonary edema.

---

#### **7. High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Hippus:** The pathognomonic "dancing pupil" sign.
- ★ **Contraction-band Necrosis:** The specific heart finding at autopsy.
- ★ **Monk’s Hood vs. Horseradish:** Aconite root is conical and shriveled; Horseradish is long, rough, and pungent.
- ★ **Consciousness:** Stays intact until death.

---

#### **8. Viva Questions**

1. **Why is Aconite called 'Wolf’s Bane'?**
    - _Ans:_ Because it was historically used to poison meat laid out for wolves.
2. **How do you differentiate Aconite and Horseradish roots?**
    - _Ans:_ Aconite is dark brown, conical, and shriveled with longitudinal wrinkles; Horseradish is pale, cylindrical, and branched.
3. **What is the specific action of Aconitine on the heart?**
    - _Ans:_ It acts on voltage-sensitive sodium channels, leading to myocardial paralysis and arrhythmias.

---

#### **Quick Revision Summary**

- **Type:** Cardiac plant poison (_Aconitum_).
- **Active Principle:** Aconitine (Alkaloid).
- **Mechanism:** Sodium channel modulator; stimulates then paralyzes nerves.
- **Classic Signs:** **Hippus**, bitter-sweet taste, numbness, arrhythmias.
- **Treatment:** Gastric lavage (Tannic acid) + Atropine + Anti-arrhythmics.
- **Medico-Legal:** Ideal homicidal agent; Section 33 BNSS (Report to police).
