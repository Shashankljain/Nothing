[[Organic irritants]]
### **Ophitoxemia (Snakebite Envenomation)**

#### **Basic Concepts**

**Introduction**  
**Ophitoxemia** characterizes the clinical spectrum of **==signs and symptoms==** resulting from the injection of venom into the body by a snakebite. While approximately 70% of snakebites are from non-venomous species, the remaining 30% are medical emergencies requiring immediate intervention.

**Definition**  
Ophitoxemia is the systemic poisoning caused by the absorption of snake venom (a complex mixture of proteins and enzymes) into the blood and lymph circulation.

**Classification of Venomous Snakes**  
Snakes are classified based on the primary toxic effect of their venom:

|**Family**|**Venom Type**|**Examples ("Big Four" in India)**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Elapidae**|**Neurotoxic**|Common Cobra, Common Krait.|
|**Viperidae**|**Vasculotoxic**|Russell's Viper, Saw-scaled Viper.|
|**Hydrophidae**|**Myotoxic**|Sea snakes.|

---

### **Medico-Legal Importance**

★ **Important Exam Point: Manner of Death**  
Snakebites are usually **accidental**, often occurring at night or during agricultural work. However, they have medico-legal significance in:

- **Homicide:** Rarely, venom may be used as a deliberate poison.
- **Negligence:** A doctor may be held liable if they fail to diagnose or treat a bite correctly according to standard protocols.
- **Addiction:** In some regions, snake venom is used for its psychoactive properties.

### **Clinical Features (Signs and Symptoms)**

The clinical presentation varies significantly depending on the species of the snake.

#### **1. Neurotoxic Bites (Elapids: Cobra/Krait)**

- **Local:** Minimal local reaction; "skip lesions" (areas of necrosis) may be seen in spitting cobras.
- **Early Signs:** **Ptosis** (bilateral drooping of eyelids) is the commonest and earliest manifestation.
- **Systemic:** Diplopia (double vision), dysphagia (difficulty swallowing), and dysarthria (difficulty talking).
- **Classic Sign:** **"Broken Neck Sign"**—The victim cannot hold their head up due to paralysis of the neck flexing muscles.
- **Terminal:** Descending paralysis leading to respiratory muscle failure and death.

#### **2. Vasculotoxic Bites (Vipers: Russell's/Saw-scaled)**

- **Local:** Extensive reaction with severe pain, swelling, bleeding,blisters with sero sanguinous fluid and gangrene.
- **Systemic:** Spontaneous bleeding from the gums, nose (epistaxis), and gastrointestinal tract. Salivation, ICH,SCH
- **Complications:** **Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)** and Acute Renal Failure.

#### **3. Myotoxic Bites (Sea Snakes)**

- **Local:** Minimal pain or swelling.
- **Systemic:** Generalized **rhabdomyolysis** (muscle breakdown), muscle tenderness, and trismus (lockjaw).
- **Sign:** **Myoglobinuria**—Passage of dark brown/tea-colored urine within 3 hours.

### **Diagnosis**

Diagnosis is primarily clinical, supported by bedside tests:

1. **20-Minute Whole Blood Clotting Test (20 WBCT):**
    - **Procedure:** Place a few mL of venous blood in a dry glass tube and wait for 20 minutes.
    - **Inference:** If the blood remains **unclotted**, it indicates vasculotoxicity (Viper bite).
2. **Single Breath Count Test:**
    - Used in Elapid bites to monitor impending respiratory failure. The patient is asked to count as far as possible in a single breath.
3. **Physical Exam:** Checking for B/L ptosis and the "broken neck sign".
4. **Dry Bite:** Approximately 15% of venomous snakebites result in no envenomation (no systemic signs), known as a **dry bite**.

---

### **Management**

#### **1. Immediate First Aid (The "RIGHT" Strategy)**

★ **Important Exam Point**

- **R (Reassure):** Most bites are non-venomous.
- **I (Immobilize):** Use a splint or sling. Movement hastens venom absorption into the lymph.
- **G (Get to Hospital):** Immediate transport is vital.
- **H (Hospital):** Reach a facility equipped with ASV.
- **T (Tell Symptoms):** Inform the doctor of any changes observed during transport.

**Mistakes to Avoid:** **NO** suction, **NO** cautery, **NO** incision, **NO** tourniquet, and **NO** ice packs.

#### **2. Hospital Management**

- **Antivenom (ASV):**
    - **Type:** Polyvalent ASV (neutralizes Cobra, Krait, Russell's Viper, and Saw-scaled Viper).
    - **Dose:** Recommended initial dose is **10 vials** administered via slow IV infusion.
- **Neurotoxic Support:**
    - **Neostigmine** (0.01–0.04 mg/kg) preceded by **Atropine** to prevent muscarinic side effects.
    - Mechanical ventilation if respiratory paralysis occurs.
- **Vasculotoxic Support:**
    - **Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)** for bleeding and DIC.
    - Dialysis for acute renal failure.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Earliest Sign of Neurotoxicity:** Ptosis.
- **Ideal Site for Blood Collection:** **Femoral vein** during autopsy to avoid postmortem contamination.
- **Commonest Fatal Snake in India:** Russell's Viper.
- **ASV Mechanism:** It is a **physiological/serological antidote**; it does not chemically destroy venom but neutralizes its action.

### **Viva Questions**

1. **What is the "Broken Neck Sign"?**
    - _Ans:_ Paralysis of the neck flexing muscles in neurotoxic (Cobra) bites, making the head fall forward.
2. **Why is a tourniquet avoided in Viper bites?**
    - _Ans:_ It can worsen local tissue necrosis and may lead to gangrene.
3. **What is the significance of the 20 WBCT?**
    - _Ans:_ It is a simple bedside test to identify vasculotoxic (Viper) bites by demonstrating incoagulable blood.
4. **Which snake bite causes myoglobinuria?**
    - _Ans:_ Sea snakes, leading to tea-colored urine within 3 hours.

1. **Types:** Elapid (Neurotoxic), Viper (Vasculotoxic), Sea Snake (Myotoxic).
2. **Symptoms:** Ptosis & "Broken Neck" (Neuro); DIC & local necrosis (Vasculo); Dark urine (Myo).
3. **Diagnosis:** 20 WBCT (Viper) and Single Breath Count (Elapid).
