### **Chronic Lead Poisoning (Plumbism/Saturnism)**

---
[[Metallic poisoning]]
#### **Basic Concepts**

**Introduction** Lead (**Shisha**) is a heavy, steel-gray metal that is ubiquitous in the environment but has **no physiological role** in the human body. Chronic lead poisoning, also known as **Plumbism** or **Saturnism**, is the most common form of heavy metal toxicity encountered in clinical and forensic practice.

**Definition** Plumbism is a clinical syndrome resulting from the cumulative absorption of lead through the gastrointestinal tract, respiratory system, or skin over a prolonged period, leading to multi-organ dysfunction.

**Classification of Lead Compounds**

1. **Inorganic Lead:** Lead oxides, metallic lead, and salts.
    - _Lead Acetate:_ Sugar of lead (formerly used as an astringent).
    - _Lead Tetraoxide:_ **Sindoor** (red lead).
    - _Lead Sulfide:_ Surma (applied to eyes).
    - _Lead Carbonate:_ White lead used in paints.
2. **Organic Lead:** Tetraethyl lead and tetramethyl lead (used as antiknock agents in petrol). Organic lead is more toxic due to its lipid solubility and primary effect on the CNS.

**Principles of Toxicokinetics**

- **Absorption:** Lead is absorbed via ingestion (food/dust), inhalation (fumes/dust), and skin contact (organic lead or tetraoxide).
- **Distribution:** 95–99% of lead in the blood is sequestered in **RBCs**. In chronic exposure, 90% is deposited in the **bones** as phosphate and carbonate.
- **Excretion:** Mainly through urine (70%), but the rate is slow, leading to cumulative toxicity.

### **Medico-Legal Importance**

★ **Important Exam Point: Manner of Exposure**

- **Occupational Hazard:** Common in painters (**Painter's Colic**), plumbers, glass blowers, battery workers, and printers.
- **Accidental:** In children who put lead-containing items in their mouths (**Pica**) or ingest lead paint dust.
- **Homicidal/Suicidal:** Rare, due to the slow onset of symptoms.
- **Abortifacient:** Lead oleate or red lead is sometimes used locally to procure criminal abortion.
- **Cattle Poison:** Used alone or mixed with arsenic to kill livestock.

### **Clinical Features (Signs and Symptoms)**

Chronic poisoning is insidious, meaning symptoms develop gradually over time.

**I. The ABCDEFGHI Mnemonic for Chronic Symptoms**

- **A:** **A**nemia / **A**norexia / **A**rthralgia / **A**bortion / **A**trophy of optic nerve.
- **B:** **B**asophilic stippling / **B**urtonian line.
- **C:** **C**olic / **C**onstipation / **C**erebral edema.
- **D:** **D**rop (**Wrist drop / Foot drop**).
- **E:** **E**ncephalopathy / **E**maciation / **E**rectile dysfunction.
- **F:** **F**acial pallor (Earliest sign) / **F**oul breath / **F**anconi syndrome.
- **G:** **G**onadal dysfunction / **G**out (Saturnine gout).
- **H:** **H**ypertension / **H**eadache / **H**allucination.
- **I:** **I**nfertility / **I**nsomnia / **I**rritability.

**II. Key Clinical Findings**

1. **Facial Pallor:** The **earliest sign** of plumbism, seen around the mouth due to vasospasm.
2. **Burton's/Burtonian Line:** A **stippled blue line** on the gingival surface (gums) found in 50–70% of cases with poor oral hygiene. It is caused by lead sulfide formation.
3. **Anemia & Punctate Basophilia:** Lead inhibits heme synthesis, leading to anemia. The diagnostic marker is **Basophilic Stippling** (dark blue spots in RBCs representing aggregated ribosomes).
4. **Saturnine Colic:** Severe, spasmodic abdominal pain that is **relieved by pressure**. It is usually preceded by constipation.
5. **Lead Palsy:** Paralysis of the extensor muscles, leading to **Wrist Drop** or **Foot Drop**. Sensory nerves are typically not affected.
6. **Lead Encephalopathy:** More common in children. Features include irritability, convulsions, delirium, coma, and death due to cerebral edema.

### **Medico-Legal Examination & Investigations**

**I. Specimen Collection**

- **Blood:** The most useful sample for recent exposure (collect from **Femoral vein** during autopsy).
- **Urine:** Used to detect recent poisoning; 24-hour samples are preferred.
- **Bone (Femur):** Best specimen for confirming long-term cumulative exposure.
- **Hair & Nails:** Helpful for detecting chronic toxicity.

**II. Laboratory Diagnosis**

- **Blood Lead Levels (BLL):** Diagnostic in adults at **>10 $\mu$g/dL** and children at **>5 $\mu$g/dL**.
- **Punctate Basophilia:** >200 cells/cu mm is a diagnostic biological marker.
- **Urine:** Elevated levels of **$\delta$-aminolevulinic acid ($\delta$-ALA)** and coproporphyrin III.
- **X-Ray:** **Lead Lines** (radio-opaque bands) at the metaphyseal plates of long bones in children.

---

### **Management**

1. **Remove from Source:** Immediate cessation of occupational or environmental exposure.
2. **Specific Antidote (Chelation Therapy):**
    - **CaNa$_2$EDTA:** The standard chelator given IV.
    - **BAL (Dimercaprol):** Chelator of choice if there is renal impairment; often used in combination with EDTA for encephalopathy.
    - **DMSA (Succimer):** An oral chelator used for mild to moderate toxicity.
3. **Supportive Care:** Mannitol for cerebral edema, and IV Diazepam for seizures.
4. **Dietary Management:** Correction of iron, calcium, and magnesium deficiencies lowers lead absorption.

---

### **Postmortem Findings**

- **External:** Emaciation, rigor mortis appears early, and a blue line on the gums (Burtonian line).
- **Internal:**
    - **Stomach:** Mucosa may show reddish discoloration (in acute tetraoxide ingestion).
    - **Muscles:** Atrophy and fatty degeneration of paralyzed muscles.
    - **Brain:** Pale, swollen with flattened gyri.
    - **Kidneys:** Contracted and hard; histopathology shows **eosinophilic intra-nuclear inclusions** in renal tubular cells.

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Earliest Sign:** Facial pallor.
- ★ **Painter's Colic:** Abdominal pain relieved by pressure, diagnostic of plumbism.
- ★ **Biological Marker:** Punctate basophilia (Basophilic stippling).
- ★ **Burtonian Line:** Blue line on gums; also seen in mercury, bismuth, and silver poisoning.
- ★ **Preferred Bone for Analysis:** Femur.

### **Viva Questions**

1. **What are 'Cabot's rings'?**
    - _Ans:_ Thread-like filaments in the shape of a figure-8 seen in Wright-stained RBCs in lead poisoning and other anemias.
2. **Why is a doctor legally bound to report Plumbism?**
    - _Ans:_ Under Section 33 BNSS, all poisoning cases must be reported to the state, as they are "Privileged Communications".
3. **What is the 'Double-condom' sign?**
    - _Ans:_ While not specific to lead, it is a radiolucent rim seen in X-rays of 'Body Packers' who swallow drug packets.
4. **What is 'Wrist Drop'?**
    - _Ans:_ Paralysis of the extensor muscles of the wrist due to lead-induced peripheral motor neuropathy.
