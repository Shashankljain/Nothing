### **Chronic Arsenic Poisoning (Arsenicosis)**
[[Metallic poisoning]]
#### **1. Basic Concepts**

**Definition**  
Chronic arsenic poisoning, also known as **Arsenicosis**, results from the ingestion of small doses of arsenic over a prolonged period. It is characterized by gradual health deterioration and specific changes in the skin, nails, and nervous system.

**Introduction**  
Arsenic is a heavy metal historically referred to as the **"Inheritance Powder"** because it was used to kill family members to gain inheritance. The most toxic form is **Arsenic Trioxide** ($As_2O_3$), which is tasteless, odorless, and sparingly soluble in water.

**Toxicokinetics**  
Arsenic is a cumulative poison. It is deposited in tissues rich in keratin, such as the **hair, nails, and skin**, and can be detected in bones for several years after death.

---

#### **2. Clinical Features**

Clinical features manifest across four distinct stages:

**I. Gastrointestinal (GIT) Disturbances**

- Symptoms: Metallic taste, excessive salivation (ptyalism), nausea, vomiting, and chronic diarrhea or constipation.

**II. Catarrhal Changes**

- Irritation of mucous membranes leading to conjunctivitis (watery eyes), running nose, and chronic cough or hoarseness.

**III. Skin and Nail Changes (★ Important Exam Point)**  
This is the most characteristic stage of chronic poisoning:

- **Raindrop Pigmentation:** Speckled brown patches of depigmentation occurring on a hyperpigmented skin base, typically on the chest, back, and neck.
- **Melanosis:** Darkening of the skin.
- **Hyperkeratosis:** Thickening and irregular skin on the palms and soles.
- **Aldrich-Mees Lines:** Transverse white bands appearing across the fingernails and toenails.
- **Blackfoot Disease:** A severe vascular complication involving peripheral vascular insufficiency and gangrene, often leading to spontaneous amputation of the feet.

**IV. Nervous Disturbances**

- **Peripheral Neuropathy:** Symmetrical tingling and numbness in the hands and feet ("Glove and Stocking" distribution).
- **Erythromelalgia (Burning Feet Syndrome):** Extreme sensitivity and pain in the legs.

**Mnemonic for Chronic Symptoms: ARSENIC**

- **A**: **A**ldrich-Mees' lines
- **R**: **R**aindrop pigmentation
- **S**: **S**oles and palms hyperkeratosis
- **E**: **E**rythromelalgia (Burning feet)
- **N**: **N**europathy (Peripheral)
- **I**: **I**cterus (Jaundice)
- **C**: **C**irrhosis

#### **3. Medico-Legal Importance**

**I. Homicidal Use**  
Arsenic was a popular homicidal poison because:

- It is cheap and easily available.
- It is tasteless, odorless, and colorless.
- Small quantities are required to cause death.
- The gradual onset of symptoms mimics natural disease (like cholera), allowing the offender to escape suspicion.

**II. Occupational and Environmental Exposure**

- **Occupational:** Workers in metal foundries, mining, glass production, and the semiconductor industry are at risk.
- **Environmental:** Chronic poisoning occurs through drinking well water contaminated with arsenic (e.g., from shallow tube wells).

**III. Other Uses**

- **Cattle Poison:** Used to kill livestock to obtain their hide.
- **Abortifacient:** Applied locally in the form of a paste to induce criminal abortion.
- **Malingering:** Powdered seeds (in some plant irritants) or arsenic can be used to produce conjunctivitis or simulate bruises.

#### **5. Medico-Legal Examination & Specimen Collection**

**Diagnosis in the Living**

- **Preferred Specimens:** Hair and nails are used for chronic cases as they show successive deposits of arsenic.
- **Laboratory Tests:** Neutron Activation Analysis (NAA) and Atomic Absorption Spectroscopy (AAS) are used to detect minute levels.

**Autopsy Findings (Chronic)**

- **External:** Emaciation (thinning), skin pigmentation, hyperkeratosis, and white streaks on nails.
- **Internal:** Chronic gastritis, fatty degeneration (cirrhosis) of the liver, and tubular necrosis of the kidneys.
- **Postmortem Imbibition:** If a body is buried in arsenic-rich soil, the poison may seep into the tissues, making it difficult to distinguish from actual poisoning.

---

#### **6. Management**

1. **Remove from Source:** Immediate discontinuation of exposure to contaminated water or occupational sources.
2. **Chelation Therapy (Specific Antidote):**
    - **BAL (British Anti-Lewisite/Dimercaprol):** 3–5 mg/kg IM 4 hourly for the first 2 days.
    - **DMSA (Succimer):** An oral alternative to BAL.
3. **Supportive Care:** Vitamin B complex for neuropathy and antioxidant therapy.
4. **Skin Decontamination:** Vigorous scrubbing with soap and water to remove arsenic from the skin.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Inheritance Powder:** Historical name for arsenic.
- ★ **Preferred Specimen:** Hair is the specimen of choice for diagnosing chronic exposure.
- ★ **Differentiating Feature:** Acute arsenic poisoning mimics **Cholera**, but in arsenic poisoning, the throat pain occurs _before_ vomiting, and tenesmus is present.
- ★ **Blackfoot Disease:** Unique vascular complication of chronic arsenic toxicity.
- ★ **Law:** Notify police under **Section 33 BNSS**.
