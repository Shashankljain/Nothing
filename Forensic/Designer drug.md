[[Drug dependance]]
### **Designer Drugs**

---

#### **1. Basic Concepts**

**Definition**  
**Designer drugs** are synthetic versions of controlled substances that are chemically modified to circumvent existing drug laws while mimicking the pharmacological effects of the original drug. In forensic medicine, they are often classified under **Substance Abuse and Drug Addiction**.

**Introduction**  
These substances are typically manufactured in clandestine laboratories. By slightly altering the molecular structure of a known illegal drug, manufacturers create a "new" compound that may not be explicitly listed in legal schedules, such as the **Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances (NDPS) Act**, until specific updates are made.

---

#### **2. Common Types and Examples**

Designer drugs often fall into categories based on the illicit drugs they mimic:

- **Synthetic Opioids:** Derivatives of drugs like **Fentanyl** and **Pethidine**. Examples include **Subutex (buprenorphine hydrochloride)**, which is frequently abused.
- **Synthetic Stimulants:** Modified versions of **Cocaine** or **Amphetamines**. These include substances like **MDMA (Ecstasy)** and "bath salts".
- **Synthetic Hallucinogens:** Chemically altered forms of **LSD** or **Phencyclidine (PCP)**.
- **Synthetic Cannabinoids:** Often referred to as "Spice" or "K2," these are designed to mimic the effects of **THC**found in Cannabis.

---

#### **3. Medico-Legal Importance**

★ **Important Exam Point: Legal Circumvention**  
The primary medico-legal challenge with designer drugs is that they are specifically engineered to be "legal" by being chemically distinct from substances named in the **NDPS Act, 1985**.

- **Criminal Responsibility:** Under **Section 22 BNS (formerly 85/86 IPC)**, crimes committed under the influence of these drugs are evaluated based on whether the intoxication was voluntary or involuntary.
- **Detection Challenges:** Because their structures are constantly changing, routine toxicological screens may fail to identify them. Advanced methods like **GC-MS (Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry)** are often required for definitive identification.
- **Reporting Obligations:** Under **Section 33 BNSS**, medical officers are mandatory reporters for all suspected poisoning cases, including those involving synthetic or unknown "designer" substances.

---

#### **4. Relevant Law (NDPS Act, 1985)**

The **NDPS Act** regulates these substances in India. Punishment is often based on the quantity found in possession:

|**Quantity Type**|**Definition/Example**|**Punishment (General)**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Small Quantity**|e.g., MDMA: 0.5 g|Up to 6 months Rigorous Imprisonment (RI) or ₹10,000 fine.|
|**Commercial Quantity**|e.g., MDMA: 10 g|10–20 years RI and ₹1–2 lakh fine.|

---

#### **5. Clinical Features and Management**

**Clinical Presentation**  
Symptoms vary widely but often include a "Toxidrome" similar to the parent drug (e.g., an **Opiate Toxidrome** for synthetic opioids or a **Sympathomimetic Toxidrome** for synthetic stimulants):

- **Stimulant types:** Tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, and paranoia.
- **Opioid types:** Respiratory depression, coma, and miosis.

**Management**  
★ **Important Exam Point: Supportive Care (ABCD)**

1. **Stabilization:** Maintain Airway, Breathing, and Circulation.
2. **Decontamination:** Gastric lavage with **KMnO₄ (1:5000)** if ingested within 1 hour.
3. **Antidotes:** Use **Naloxone** for synthetic opioid toxicity. No specific physiological antidotes exist for most synthetic stimulants; symptoms like seizures are managed with **Diazepam**.

---

#### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Definition:** Chemically modified versions of controlled drugs to evade laws.
- ★ **Detection:** Requires sensitive instrumentation like **GC-MS**.
- ★ **Law:** Possession is a criminal offence under the **NDPS Act, 1985**.
- ★ **Reporting:** Mandatory under **Section 33 BNSS**.

#### **Viva Questions**

1. **What is "Speed-balling"?**
    - _Ans:_ It is a mixture of cocaine (a stimulant) and an opioid (a depressant).
2. **Why are designer drugs dangerous?**
    - _Ans:_ Their potency is often much higher than natural drugs, and their exact chemical composition is unknown, making treatment difficult.
3. **Which act regulates these drugs in India?**
    - _Ans:_ The Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances (NDPS) Act, 1985.
