[[Spinal poison]]
This answer is designed for university examinations, focusing on the critical clinical and forensic differences between strychnine poisoning and tetanus.

### **1. Basic Concepts**

**Introduction to Strychnine Poisoning**  
Strychnine is a **spinal neurotic poison** obtained from the seeds of _Strychnos nux-vomica_. It acts by competitively antagonising **glycine**, an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the spinal cord, leading to uncontrolled muscle contractions.

**Introduction to Tetanus**  
Tetanus is a bacterial infection caused by _**Clostridium tetani**_. It results in muscular rigidity and spasms due to the release of tetanospasmin toxin, which also affects the nervous system but follows a different clinical course than poisoning.

---

### **2. Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Differential Diagnosis:** It is vital for a medical practitioner to differentiate these two conditions as the treatment and legal implications differ drastically.
- **Role in Criminal Investigation:** Strychnine is sometimes used as a homicidal agent, though its bitter taste makes this rare.
- **Importance for Medical Practitioners:** Failure to distinguish poisoning from natural disease can lead to allegations of medical negligence or failure to report a crime.

---

### **3. Relevant Law (BNS & BNSS, 2023)**

University exams require the application of the new 2023 criminal codes for all poisoning cases:

|**Act**|**Section**|**Provision**|**Medico-Legal Significance**|
|:--|:--|:--|:--|
|**BNSS**|**33**|Information to Police|**Mandatory** for doctors to report all suspected poisoning cases to the police.|
|**BNS**|**123**|Hurt by Poison|Administering poison with intent to cause hurt or commit a crime.|
|**BNS**|**101**|Punishment for Murder|Applicable if strychnine administration leads to death.|
|**BNS**|**211**|Non-compliance|Punishment for doctors failing to report a mandatory poisoning case.|

### **4. Detailed Comparison Table**

★ **Important Exam Point: This table is a frequent university question.**

|**Feature**|**Strychnine Poisoning**|**Tetanus (Lockjaw)**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**History of Injury**|No history of injury.|Usually present (e.g., rusty nail prick).|
|**Onset**|**Sudden** (within minutes or hours).|**Gradual** (several days).|
|**First Symptoms**|Bitter taste and choking sensation.|**Trismus (Lockjaw)** starts first.|
|**Lockjaw (Trismus)**|Starts late or simultaneously with other muscles.|Starts first and specifically affects the lower jaw.|
|**Muscular State**|**Relaxed completely** between convulsions.|**Rigid throughout**; no complete relaxation.|
|**Convulsions**|Triggered by the slightest external stimuli.|Usually more spontaneous or constant rigidity.|
|**Mental State**|**Fully conscious** and aware during seizures.|Generally conscious, but less acute fear.|
|**Fatal Period**|Very short (**1–2 hours**).|Longer (**> 24 hours**).|
|**Chemical Analysis**|Strychnine found in blood, urine, or viscera.|No poison found.|
|**Culture**|No bacterial growth.|Culture shows _Clostridium tetani_.|

---

### **5. Post-Mortem Examination Findings**

If the victim dies, the following are observed:

- **Strychnine:** Early onset of rigor mortis and potential presence of **cadaveric spasm** due to the body 'freezing' in the middle of a convulsion. Viscera are congested, and strychnine is detectable even in decomposed bodies.
- **Tetanus:** No characteristic post-mortem findings other than those of the original injury/wound.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Risus Sardonicus:** The characteristic "evil grin" caused by jaw muscle contraction; seen in both, but starts earlier in tetanus.
- ★ **Opisthotonus:** Backwards arching of the body; a hallmark of spinal neurotic poisoning.
- ★ **Conscious Seizures:** The single most important clinical sign of strychnine is that the patient remains awake.
- ★ **Antidote:** Barbiturates (Sodium Amytal) are used for strychnine, whereas tetanus requires toxoids and supportive care.

### **Viva Questions**

1. **Why is the muscular state relaxed between strychnine convulsions?**
    - _Ans:_ Because it is a competitive inhibition of glycine; once the stimulus fades, the muscles can briefly relax until the next impulse.
2. **What is the fatal dose of strychnine?**
    - _Ans:_ 15–50 mg or even **one crushed seed**.
3. **Which act makes reporting this poisoning mandatory?**
    - _Ans:_ Section 33 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023.
