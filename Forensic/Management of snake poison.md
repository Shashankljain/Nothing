### **Management of Snakebite (Ophitoxemia)**
[[Organic irritants]]
---

#### **Basic Concepts**

**Definition**  
**Ophitoxemia** is the clinical spectrum of signs and symptoms resulting from the systemic injection of snake venom into the body through a bite.

**Introduction**  
Snakebite is a common medical emergency, particularly in tropical regions like India. While there are many species of snakes, management primarily focuses on the "Big Four" venomous snakes: **Common Cobra, Common Krait, Russell’s Viper, and Saw-scaled Viper**.

**Principles of Management**

1. **Life-saving First Aid:** Preventing further absorption of venom.
2. **Diagnosis:** Identifying systemic envenomation (neurotoxic vs. vasculotoxic).
3. **Antivenom Therapy:** Neutralizing the circulating venom.
4. **Supportive Care:** Managing complications like respiratory failure or renal damage.

---

#### **Medico-Legal Importance**

★ **Important Exam Point: Manner of Death**

- **Accidental:** The most common manner, often occurring while farming or sleeping.
- **Homicidal:** Rare; involves using a snake to kill a person (e.g., throwing a snake on a sleeping victim).
- **Suicidal:** Extremely rare (e.g., historical accounts like Cleopatra).
- **Negligence:** Allegations may be leveled against a doctor for failing to identify the snake or delay in starting ASV.
- **Addiction:** Venom is sometimes used as a recreational drug for euphoric effects.

#### **Management Protocol**

#### **I. Immediate First Aid (The "RIGHT" Strategy)**

★ **Important Exam Point: RIGHT Mnemonic**

- **R (Reassure):** Reassure the victim as 70–80% of bites are from non-venomous snakes.
- **I (Immobilize):** Immobilize the bitten limb using a splint or sling. Movement hastens venom absorption into the lymphatics.
- **G (Get to Hospital):** Transport the victim immediately to a medical facility.
- **H (Hospital):** Reach a hospital where Antivenom (ASV) is available.
- **T (Tell Symptoms):** Inform the doctor of any changes (e.g., drooping eyelids) noted during transport.

> [!danger] **Mistakes to Avoid (Strictly Contraindicated)** **NO** Incision (cutting) over the bite, **NO** Suction (sucking venom by mouth), **NO** Tourniquets (causes necrosis), **NO** Ice packs, **NO** Electric shock.

#### **II. Pressure Immobilization (Sutherland Wrap)**

- **Indication:** Recommended for **Elapid** (Cobra/Krait) and **Sea snake** bites.
- **Method:** A crepe bandage is wrapped firmly (50–70 mmHg) from the bite site upwards to occlude lymphatics.
- **Contraindication:** Do **NOT** use for **Viper** bites as it increases local tissue necrosis and risk of **Compartment Syndrome**.

#### **III. Hospital Management & Diagnosis**

1. **20-Minute Whole Blood Clotting Test (20 WBCT):**
    - **How:** Place 2 mL of fresh venous blood in a dry glass tube and wait 20 minutes.
    - **Inference:** If the blood remains **unclotted**, it indicates vasculotoxicity (Viper bite).
2. **Single Breath Counting Test:**
    - The patient is asked to count numbers in a single breath. A falling count indicates impending respiratory failure in neurotoxic bites.

#### **IV. Antivenom Treatment (ASV)**

★ **High-Yield Exam Point: ASV Administration**

- **Type:** Polyvalent ASV (neutralizes the "Big Four").
- **Dose:** Recommended initial dose is **8–10 vials**.
- **Route:** Administered via slow IV infusion in 200–500 mL of isotonic saline.
- **Children:** They must receive the **EXACT same dose** as adults because snakes inject the same amount of venom regardless of the victim's size.
- **Adverse Reactions:** Be prepared for anaphylaxis or serum sickness.

#### **V. Supportive & Syndromic Management**

1. **Neurotoxic Bites (Cobra/Krait):**
    - **Neostigmine:** 1.5 mg IM/IV to improve muscular weakness.
    - ★ **Atropine Pre-medication:** 0.6 mg IV **must** be given before neostigmine to block unwanted muscarinic side effects.
    - **Ventilation:** Mechanical ventilation if respiratory failure occurs.
2. **Vasculotoxic Bites (Viper):**
    - **FFP:** Fresh Frozen Plasma for bleeding disorders.
    - **Dialysis:** For acute renal failure.
3. **Myotoxic Bites (Sea Snake):**
    - Manage hyperkalemia and provide intensive hydration for rhabdomyolysis.

---

#### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Dry Bite:** Occurs in about 20% of venomous bites where the snake fails to inject venom.
- **Ideal Autopsy Sample:** **Femoral vein blood** should be preserved to avoid postmortem contamination from the stomach [History].
- **PAPPA Contraindication:** Rectified spirit is used to preserve viscera for most poisons, EXCEPT for **P**henol, **A**lcohol, **P**hosphorus, **P**araldehyde, and **A**cetic acid [History]. It is safe for snakebite viscera.
- **Broken Neck Sign:** Diagnostic of neurotoxic paralysis of the neck flexing muscles.

#### **Viva Questions**

1. **Why is the ASV dose the same for children and adults?**
    - _Ans:_ ASV neutralizes a fixed quantity of venom. Since snakes inject the same amount of venom into children as they do in adults, the required dose of antivenom remains the same.
2. **What is the "Broken Neck Sign"?**
    - _Ans:_ It is a sign of neurotoxic envenoming where paralysis of neck flexing muscles prevents the patient from holding their head up.
3. **Why is a tourniquet avoided in Viper bites?**
    - _Ans:_ It can trap vasculotoxic venom locally, leading to severe necrosis, gangrene, and potentially compartment syndrome.
4. **What is the 20 WBCT?**
    - _Ans:_ A simple bedside test to identify vasculotoxicity (Viper bite) by demonstrating incoagulable blood.

#### **Quick Revision Summary (One-Page Summary)**

- **Snake ID:** 2 fang marks (poisonous), broad belly scales, small head scales (except Cobra/Krait).
- **First Aid:** RIGHT strategy; No incision/suction.
- **Sutherland Wrap:** For Elapids only; Contraindicated for Vipers.
- **Diagnosis:** 20 WBCT (Vipers); Single breath count (Elapids).
- **ASV:** 10 vials polyvalent ASV via slow IV; same dose for children.
- **Supportive:** Neostigmine + Atropine (Elapids); FFP/Dialysis (Vipers).
- **Law:** Notify police under **Section 33 BNSS**.
