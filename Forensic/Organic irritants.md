**Irritants and Snakebite Management**

---

**I. Classification of Irritants**

**Definition** Irritants are substances that cause inflammation, irritation, or ulceration of the tissues upon contact. Unlike corrosives, their symptoms are usually manifested more slowly.

**Classification and Examples**:

|**Category**|**Sub-category**|**Examples**|
|---|---|---|
|**1. Inorganic**|**Metallic**|Arsenic, Antimony, Copper, Lead, Mercury, Zinc.|
||**Non-metallic**|Phosphorus, Chlorine, Iodine, Carbon tetrachloride (CCl4​).|
|**2. Organic**|**Plant**|_Abrus precatorius_ (Rati), Castor, Croton, Calotropis, Marking nut.|
||**Animal**|Snakes, Scorpions, Cantharides (Blister beetle), Spiders.|
|**3. Mechanical**||Powdered glass, chopped hair, diamond dust, needles.|

---

**II. Differences Between Poisonous and Non-Poisonous Snakes**

★ **Important Exam Point: The Big Four** In India, most venomous bites are caused by the "Big Four": Common Cobra, Common Krait, Russell’s Viper, and Saw-scaled Viper.

|**Feature**|**Poisonous (Venomous) Snake**|**Non-Poisonous Snake**|
|---|---|---|
|**Head Scales**|Usually small (except Cobras/Kraits which have large shields).|Usually large.|
|**Belly Scales**|Large and cover the **entire breadth** of the belly.|Small; do not cover the entire breadth.|
|**Tail**|Compressed/flat in sea snakes (venomous).|Round and tapering.|
|**Fangs**|Long, hollow, and **canalized** (like a hypodermic needle).|Short and solid.|
|**Bite Mark**|**Two distinct fang marks** (punctate marks).|Multiple small teeth marks in a semicircular row.|
|**Habit**|Mostly nocturnal.|Diurnal.|

**III. Management of a Poisonous Snake Bite**

**1. Immediate First Aid (The "Do it RIGHT" Mnemonic)**

- **R (Reassurance):** Reassure the victim as 70% of bites are from non-venomous snakes.
- **I (Immobilize):** Immobilize the bitten limb with a splint or sling. Movement hastens venom absorption.
- **G (Get to Hospital):** Transport the victim to a medical facility immediately.
- **T (Tell Doctor):** Inform the doctor of any systemic symptoms that developed during transport.

**2. Prevention of Venom Spread**

- **Pressure Immobilization (Sutherland Wrap):** Recommended for **Elapid** (Cobra/Krait) and sea snake bites. Use an elastic bandage (50–70 mm Hg) from the bite site upwards.
- ★ **Contraindication:** Do **NOT** use pressure immobilization for **Viper** bites as it may worsen local tissue necrosis.

**3. Hospital Management & Diagnosis**

- **20-minute Whole Blood Clotting Test (20 WBCT):** A vital bedside test for vipers. If blood remains unclotted after 20 minutes, it indicates vasculotoxicity.
- **Single Breath Counting Test:** Used to monitor impending respiratory failure in Elapid (neurotoxic) bites.

**4. Antivenom Treatment (ASV)**

- **Type:** Polyvalent Antisnake Venom (ASV) is used in India, which neutralizes the "Big Four".
- **Dose:** The recommended initial dose is **10 vials** administered via slow IV infusion.
- **Indications:** Systemic envenoming (e.g., ptosis, bleeding, shock) or rapid extension of local swelling.

**5. Supportive Care**

- **Neurotoxic (Cobra):** If muscarinic symptoms appear, use **Neostigmine** (preceded by **Atropine**).
- **Respiratory Support:** Endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation if the "Broken Neck Sign" or respiratory paralysis occurs.

**High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Broken Neck Sign:** Seen in neurotoxic bites (Cobra) when neck flexing muscles are paralyzed.
- **Ptosis:** Bilateral drooping of eyelids; the commonest and earliest sign of neuroparalytic snakebite.
- **Ideal Site for Blood Collection:** **Femoral vein** during autopsy to avoid contamination.
- **"Dry Bite":** Occurs when a venomous snake fails to inject venom (most common in cobras).

**Viva Questions**

1. **Which snake venom is myotoxic?**
    - _Ans:_ Sea snake venom.
2. **What is the "Broken Neck Sign"?**
    - _Ans:_ It is a sign of neurotoxicity where the victim cannot hold their head up due to neck muscle paralysis.
3. **Why is ASV dosage the same for children and adults?**
    - _Ans:_ Because snakes inject the same amount of venom regardless of the victim's size.
4. **Can you do an incision and suction on a bite?**
    - _Ans:_ No, it is strictly contraindicated as it causes more harm and introduces infection.

**Quick Revision Summary**

1. **Irritants:** Divided into Inorganic (Arsenic, Phosphorus), Organic (Abrus, Snakes), and Mechanical (Glass).
2. **Snake ID:** Broad belly scales and two fang marks identify a poisonous snake.
3. **Management:** Reassurance, immobilization, and **10 vials of Polyvalent ASV**.
4. **Law:** Section 33 BNSS (Report to police) and Section 123 BNS (Hurt by poison).
