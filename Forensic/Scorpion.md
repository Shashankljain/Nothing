**Scorpion Sting (Bichu)**
[[Organic irritants]]
#### **1. Basic Concepts**

**Definition**  
Scorpions are eight-legged venomous arachnids. A scorpion sting involves the injection of venom through a stinger located at the end of its tail.

**Introduction**  
There are approximately 100 species of scorpions in India. The most clinically significant is the **Indian Red Scorpion**(_Mesobuthus tamulus_), which is responsible for severe cases, including acute pulmonary edema.

**Principles of Toxicology**

- **Venom Properties:** The venom is a clear, colorless, proteinous toxalbumin. It contains both **neurotoxins** and **hemotoxins**.
- **Mechanism of Action:** It affects $Na^+ - K^+$ channels and acts as a potent autonomic stimulator. This leads to an **"Autonomic Storm"**—an uncontrolled release of catecholamines (epinephrine/norepinephrine) from the adrenal glands.

---

#### **2. Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Accidental Exposure:** Most cases are accidental, occurring in rural or agricultural settings.
- **Negligence:** Failure to monitor a patient for systemic envenomation (like pulmonary edema) can lead to allegations of medical negligence.
- **Homicide/Suicide:** These are extremely rare for scorpion stings.

#### **4. Clinical Features**

The symptoms are divided into local and systemic effects.

**Local Symptoms**

- **Intense Pain:** Radiating and burning in nature.
- **Tap Test:** Pain is characteristically accentuated by tapping over the affected area.
- **Findings:** Swelling, blisters, and reddening. Unlike a snakebite (two holes), a scorpion sting usually shows **one central hole**.

**Systemic Symptoms (The Autonomic Storm)**

- **Sympathetic Overactivity:** Sweating, tachycardia (rapid heart rate), palpitations, hypertension, and **priapism**(painful, persistent erection).
- **Parasympathetic Activity:** Salivation, lacrimation, rhinorrhea, and slow pulse.
- **Serious Complications:** Acute pulmonary edema, cardiac failure (especially in children), and rhabdomyolysis.

---

#### **5. Management**

★ **Important Exam Point: Prazosin** Prazosin is the physiological antagonist and the drug of choice for managing the "autonomic storm".

**Immediate Management**

- **Immobilization:** Keep the bitten limb still to slow venom spread.
- **Pain Relief:** Use NSAIDs (like Ibuprofen) or local anesthetic infiltration.

**Medical Management**

- **Prazosin Therapy:** Administered at **30 $\mu$g/kg/dose** (1 mg for adults). It is repeated every 3 hours until extremities become warm and peripheral veins are visible.
- **Scorpion Antivenom (SAV):** Specific antivenom for the Indian Red Scorpion is available. Recovery is most effective when SAV is used alongside Prazosin.
- **Supportive Care:**
    - **Atropine:** Used specifically to prevent pulmonary edema.
    - **Calcium Gluconate:** Given IV for muscle cramps and pain.

---

#### **6. Postmortem Findings**

- **External:** The sting site is swollen, and the sting may still be found in the skin. The area may show bruising (ecchymosis).
- **Internal:** Evidence of **pulmonary edema** and myocardial infarction (heart muscle damage).

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Drug of Choice:** Prazosin.
- **Mnemonic for Symptoms (Tap Test):** If tapping the site makes them scream, it's a scorpion.
- **Common Fatal Complication:** Acute pulmonary edema.
- **Priapism:** A unique systemic sign of sympathetic stimulation in these cases.

---

### **Quick Revision Summary**

1. **Species:** Indian Red Scorpion (_Mesobuthus tamulus_) is the most dangerous.
2. **Mechanism:** "Autonomic Storm" due to catecholamine release.
3. **Key Signs:** Radiating pain, positive **Tap Test**, sweating, and **priapism**.
4. **Treatment:** **Prazosin (30 $\mu$g/kg)** + Scorpion Antivenom (SAV).
5. **Duty:** Mandatory reporting to police under **Section 33 BNSS**.
