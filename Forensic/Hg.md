### **Chronic Mercurial Poisoning (Hydrargyrism)**
[[Metallic poisoning]]
---

#### **1. Basic Concepts**

**Introduction**  
**Chronic Mercurial Poisoning**, also known as **Hydrargyrism**, results from long-term exposure to mercury. It can occur through continuous accidental absorption in occupational settings, excessive therapeutic use, or following recovery from a large acute dose.

**Definition**  
It is the clinical syndrome resulting from the cumulative toxic effects of mercury (elemental, inorganic, or organic) on the body over a prolonged period.

**Forms of Mercury Involved**

- **Elemental Mercury ($Hg$):** Vapor is highly toxic to the brain when inhaled.
- **Inorganic Salts:** Primarily affect the gut and kidneys.
- **Organic Mercury:** Most toxic form (e.g., methyl mercury); widely distributed and crosses the blood-brain barrier.

**Historical Significance**  
It is famously associated with the **"Mad Hatter"** syndrome, where 18th–19th century hat makers developed tremors and irritability due to the use of mercuric nitrate in felt making.

---

#### **2. Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Occupational Hazard:** Common in workers in thermometer factories, mirror manufacturing, and hat industries.
- **Accidental Poisoning:** Can occur in children swallowing elemental mercury from broken thermometers.
- **Homicide/Suicide:** These are rare for the chronic form but have been reported via deliberate injections.
- **Environmental Disaster:** **Minamata disease** is a classic example of mass chronic poisoning caused by eating fish contaminated with industrial methyl mercury.

★ **Important Exam Point: Reporting**  
Poisoning is a **Privileged Communication**. Under **33 BNSS**, a doctor is legally bound to inform the police of any suspected case, overriding patient confidentiality.

#### **4. Clinical Features**

Chronic intoxication is characterized by a diagnostic **triad**: **Tremors**, **Neuropsychiatric disturbances**, and **Gingivostomatitis**.

**I. The Classic Triad**

1. **Intention Tremors (Danbury Tremors):**
    - Starts in the hands (affecting writing), then progresses to the lips and tongue, and finally the legs.
    - Known as "Hatter’s Shakes" or "Glass Blower’s Shakes".
2. **Mercurial Erethism:**
    - Psychiatric symptoms including extreme shyness, irritability, anxiety, and "frequent blushing".
    - The patient may have sudden attacks of anger or "explosive irritability".
3. **Gingivostomatitis:**
    - Inflammation of the gums, excessive salivation (ptyalism), and a **blue line on the gums**.
    - Loosening of teeth may occur.

**II. Specific Conditions**

- **Mercurialentis:** A peculiar eye change where a **malt-brown reflex** is seen on the anterior lens capsule due to mercury vapor deposits.
- **Acrodynia (Pink Disease):** Seen in children as an allergic reaction. Features include pinkish rashes, sweating, tachycardia, and "pain in the extremities".
- **Minamata Disease:** Results from organic mercury. Symptoms include ataxia, visual field constriction, hearing loss, and "clouding of consciousness".

---

#### **5. Medico-Legal Examination (Autopsy Findings)**

- **External:** Emaciated body, pale skin, and **bluish-gray lines** on the gums.
- **Brain:** Gyri of both hemispheres are usually **atrophic** with widened sulci, especially in the calcarine cortex.
- **Internal Organs:** Chronic nephritis (kidney damage) and potentially cerebral infarctions.

---

#### **6. Investigations**

|**Specimen**|**Toxic Level**|**Significance**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Blood**|**>10 $\mu$g/dL**|Indicates recent/ongoing exposure.|
|**Urine**|**>20 $\mu$g/L**|Best for elemental/inorganic mercury assay.|
|**Hair**|**>5 ppm**|Indicates chronic toxicity.|

- **Neutron Activation Analysis (NAA):** The best technique for assessing mercury concentration in hair.

---

#### **7. Management**

1. **Removal from Source:** Immediate discontinuation of occupational or environmental exposure.
2. **Chelation Therapy (Specific Antidote):**
    - **N-acetyl penicillamine:** The chelator of choice for chronic poisoning.
    - **DMSA (Succimer):** Useful for increasing mercury excretion.
    - ★ **Note:** **BAL (Dimercaprol)** is used for **acute** poisoning but may be less preferred in some chronic scenarios.
3. **Supportive Care:** Oral hygiene for gingivitis and demulcent drinks.

---

#### **8. High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Mnemonic for Symptoms (HG-MERCURI):** **H**atter's shakes, **G**ingivostomatitis, **M**ercurialentis, **E**rethism, **R**enal changes, **C**erebral palsy (organic), **U**rticaria, **R**ashes (Pink disease), **I**ntention tremors.
- **Specimen of Choice:** **Urine** is preferred for assaying elemental/inorganic mercury.
- **Danbury Tremors:** Progresses from hands $\rightarrow$ lips $\rightarrow$ tongue $\rightarrow$ legs.
- **Pink Disease:** Another name for Acrodynia in children.

#### **9. Viva Questions**

1. **What is the "Mad Hatter" syndrome?**
    - _Ans:_ Chronic mercury poisoning in hat makers characterized by tremors and erethism.
2. **Why is the brain opened first in poisoning autopsies?**
    - _Ans:_ To detect unusual odors before they are masked by odors from other body cavities.
3. **What is the blue line on gums called when caused by lead?**
    - _Ans:_ **Burtonian line**. A similar blue line is seen in chronic mercury poisoning.
4. **What is the specific chelator for chronic mercury?**
    - _Ans:_ **N-acetyl penicillamine**.
