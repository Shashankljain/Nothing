# **Aspirin Poisoning (Salicylism)**

### **Basic Concepts**

**Definition** **Salicylism** is the clinical state of toxicity resulting from an overdose of aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) or other salicylate-containing compounds.

**Introduction** Aspirin is a widely used analgesic and antipyretic drug. While it is safe at therapeutic levels, it can cause severe toxicity through:

1. **Acute Poisoning:** An excessive single dose taken over a short interval.
2. **Chronic Poisoning:** Smaller repeated doses that accumulate over time until they reach a toxic threshold.
3. **Idiosyncrasy:** A state of inherent hypersensitivity where even therapeutic doses produce toxic symptoms in certain individuals.

---

### **Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Reporting Obligations:** Under **Section 33 BNSS (2023)**, a medical practitioner is legally required to report all suspected poisoning cases to the police.
- **Idiosyncrasy & Hypersensitivity:** Medico-legally significant because toxic reactions (like bowel hemorrhage or dyspnoea) can occur in sensitive patients even without an "overdose" in the traditional sense.
- **Suicide:** Aspirin is sometimes chosen as an agent for self-harm due to its easy availability.
- **Accidental:** Common in children or through the ingestion of multiple medications containing salicylates.

★ **Important Exam Point: Reporting** Failure to report a poisoning case is a punishable offence under **Section 211 BNS (2023)**.

### **Topic-Specific Explanation**

**Mechanism / Pathophysiology**

1. **Metabolic Acidosis:** Salicylates are a primary cause of **high-anion gap metabolic acidosis**.
2. **Cumulative Effect:** Aspirin is not always excreted readily and can accumulate in the body when given in repeated doses, leading to toxicity once a threshold is reached.
3. **Gastric Bezoars:** In rare cases of massive ingestion, aspirin can coalesce into "bezoars" or gastric concentrations in the stomach, which may fail to pass through the GIT and require surgical or endoscopic removal.

---

### **Clinical Features**

**I. Signs and Symptoms**

- **General:** Increased perspiration (sweating).
- **Gastrointestinal:** Nausea, vomiting, and in severe cases, **hemorrhage from the bowel**.
- **Respiratory:** Dyspnoea (difficulty breathing).
- **Renal:** Albuminuria (presence of albumin in urine).
- **Constitutional:** Fever and rigors.

**II. Features of Chronic Poisoning**

- Malaise, cachexia, depression, and a gradual deterioration of the patient's general condition.

---

### **Investigations**

- **Quantitative Analysis:** This is the most important proof of poisoning. Quantitative analysis of **salicylate levels** in blood and urine helps determine the severity of exposure and guides treatment.
- **Acid-Base Status:** Assessment of blood gases to identify high-anion gap metabolic acidosis.

---

### **Management**

★ **Important Exam Point: Management follows the ABCD (Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Drugs) protocol.**

1. **Gastric Decontamination:**
    - **Activated Charcoal:** Used as a physical/mechanical antidote. It is a fine black powder that adsorbs the poison onto its surface, preventing absorption into the bloodstream.
2. **Enhanced Elimination:**
    - **Alkaline Diuresis:** By administering sodium bicarbonate to make the urine alkaline (pH $\ge$ 7.5), the excretion of salicylates is significantly enhanced through ion trapping in the renal tubules.
    - **Hemodialysis:** This is indicated and highly effective for removing salicylates from the blood in cases of severe poisoning.
3. **Supportive Care:** Maintaining electrolyte balance, correcting acidosis, and providing respiratory support if needed.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **High-Anion Gap Acidosis:** Hallmark laboratory finding in salicylate poisoning.
- ★ **Alkaline Diuresis:** The specific method of choice for enhancing aspirin excretion.
- ★ **Idiosyncrasy:** Important clinical concept where standard doses cause toxicity due to inherent hypersensitivity.
- ★ **Section 33 BNSS:** Mandatory legal reporting requirement for doctors.
