[[Drug dependance]]
Although modern medical classifications (like the **ICD-11** found in the sources) focus on the term **Substance Dependence** or **Disorders due to substance use**, university examinations often require a distinction between the older terms **Drug Addiction** and **Drug Habituation**.

### **Comparison: Drug Addiction vs. Drug Habituation**

★ **Important Exam Point:** The primary difference lies in the **compulsion** to take the drug and the presence of **physical dependence**.

|**Feature**|**Drug Addiction**|**Drug Habituation**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Desire/Craving**|There is an **overpowering compulsion** or irresistible craze to take the drug.|There is a **desire** (but not a compulsion) to take the drug for a sense of well-being.|
|**Tolerance**|There is a marked tendency to **increase the dose** to get the same effect.|There is **little or no tendency** to increase the dose.|
|**Dependence**|Both **Psychic and Physical** dependence are present.|Only **Psychic** dependence is present; no physical adaptation.|
|**Withdrawal**|Sudden stoppage leads to a severe **Withdrawal Syndrome** (e.g., 'the shakes' or 'rum fits').|Sudden stoppage causes **mild irritation** but no serious physical illness.|
|**Effect on Society**|It is detrimental to **both the individual and society**(leads to crime/violence).|The detrimental effects, if any, are primarily on the **individual only**.|

---

### **Detailed Concepts**

#### **1. Drug Addiction (Substance Dependence)**

As described in the sources regarding alcohol and opioids, addiction is a chronic, progressive disease.

- **Compulsion:** Characterised by a "morbid craving" or "irresistible impulse" (e.g., Cocainomania).
- **Physical Dependence:** The body's functions are so adapted to the drug that its absence causes physiological distress.
- **Tolerance:** The body requires increasingly higher amounts. For example, in heroin addicts, the dose can be increased more than **100 times** the original dose.

#### **2. Drug Habituation**

The sources refer to "non-habituated persons" as those who have not developed a regular pattern of use or metabolic tolerance.

- It is a condition resulting from the **repeated consumption** of a drug (like tea, coffee, or mild tobacco use).
- The user can stop if they choose to, though they may feel a mental "miss" or craving.

---

### **Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Criminal Responsibility:** Under **Section 22 BNS** (formerly 84/85 IPC), an addict is generally held responsible for their acts unless the intoxication was involuntary. However, states like **"Run-amok"** (cannabis) or **Delirium Tremens** (alcohol) are considered disorders of the mind where the person may not be held responsible.
- **Professional Misconduct:** A medical practitioner who is a drug addict can have their name **erased** from the medical register by the State Medical Council for "infamous conduct".
- **NDPS Act, 1985:** This act strictly regulates addictive substances (Cocaine, Opium, Cannabis). Possession of even "small quantities" for personal use is a punishable offence, though addicts volunteering for treatment may enjoy **immunity from prosecution**.

---

### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Definition of Tolerance:** Same dose no longer produces the original effect.
- ★ **Definition of Addiction:** Compulsion + Tolerance + Physical/Psychic Dependence + Social Harm.
- ★ **Key Difference:** Habituation lacks the "Physical Dependence" and "Compulsion" seen in addiction.

### **Quick Revision Summary**

1. **Addiction:** I **MUST** have it (Compulsion); My body **needs** it (Physical Dependence); I need **more** (Tolerance).
2. **Habituation:** I **LIKE** to have it (Desire); I **think** I need it (Psychic Dependence); I use the **same** amount.
3. **Law:** Both are subject to the **NDPS Act** if the substance is a controlled drug.
