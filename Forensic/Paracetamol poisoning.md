# **Paracetamol Poisoning (Acetaminophen Toxicity)**

### **Basic Concepts**

**Definition**  
**Paracetamol poisoning** occurs due to an acute or chronic overdose of acetaminophen (paracetamol), a common over-the-counter analgesic and antipyretic drug. It is primarily classified as a **Hepatotoxic Systemic Poison** because its most severe effect is on the liver.

**Introduction**  
Paracetamol is safe at therapeutic doses, but in high quantities, it produces a toxic metabolite that causes massive damage to liver cells. It is one of the most common causes of drug-induced liver failure worldwide.

**Classification**  
According to its mode of action, it falls under:

- **Systemic Poison:** Affects the body's systems after absorption.
- **Miscellaneous:** Often grouped with other analgesics/antipyretics.
- **Organ-specific:** Specifically a **Hepatotoxic poison** (causes liver necrosis).

---

### **Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Accidental Poisoning:** Very common in children who ingest "sweet" syrups or by adults who unintentionally exceed the maximum daily dose by taking multiple preparations (e.g., cold/flu meds and pain meds) containing paracetamol.
- **Suicidal Poisoning:** Frequently used as a suicidal agent due to its easy availability and "cheap" cost.
- **Idiosyncrasy:** Some individuals show an inherent hypersensitivity to paracetamol even at therapeutic doses, leading to symptoms like dyspnoea or bowel hemorrhage.
- **Reporting:** Under **Section 33 BNSS**, a medical officer is legally bound to inform the police of any suspected poisoning case.

★ **Important Exam Point:** Failure to report a poisoning case is a punishable offence under **Section 211 BNS**.

### **Toxicokinetics and Pathophysiology**

**Mechanism of Toxicity (How it works):**

1. **Metabolism:** Paracetamol is normally metabolized in the liver. A small portion is converted into a highly toxic metabolite called **NAPQI** (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine).
2. **Glutathione Depletion:** In a normal state, NAPQI is neutralized by **glutathione**. In an overdose, glutathione stores are exhausted.
3. **Liver Necrosis:** The free NAPQI binds to liver cell proteins, causing **acute liver necrosis** (specifically centrilobular necrosis).

---

### **Clinical Features**

Toxicity often presents in stages:

- **Early Phase:** Nausea, vomiting, pallor, and diaphoresis (sweating).
- **Late Phase (24–72 hours):** Right upper quadrant pain, **jaundice** (yellowing of skin/eyes), and signs of hepatic encephalopathy (confusion, coma).
- **Renal Effects:** Can also cause **Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)** leading to renal failure.

---

### **Management**

★ **Important Exam Point: Management follows the ABCD protocol.**

1. **Decontamination:**
    - **Gastric Lavage:** Performed with warm water if the patient arrives within 1–2 hours of ingestion.
    - **Activated Charcoal:** Acts as a physical antidote by adsorbing the drug on its surface to prevent absorption.
2. **Specific Antidote:**
    - **N-Acetylcysteine (NAC):** This is the **physiological/pharmacological antidote** of choice.
    - **Action:** It replenishes glutathione stores to neutralize the toxic NAPQI metabolite.
3. **Supportive Treatment:** IV fluids to maintain hydration and glucose to treat hypoglycemia.
4. **Definitive Care:** In cases of fulminant hepatic failure, a **liver transplant** may be the only lifesaving option.

---

### **Autopsy Findings**

- **External:** Icterus (jaundice) may be visible.
- **Liver (Primary Finding):** Swollen, soft, and yellowish. Histopathology shows **acute liver necrosis**.
- **Kidneys:** May show **acute tubular necrosis** (ATN).
- **Stomach:** May contain drug fragments if death was rapid.
