### **Hatter’s Shakes (Danbury Tremors)**
[[Metallic poisoning]]
[[Hg]]
---

#### **Basic Concepts**

**Definition**  
**Hatter’s shakes**, also known as **Danbury tremors** or **Shaking Palsy**, are the characteristic intention tremors seen in **Chronic Mercurial Poisoning (Hydrargyrism)**.

**Introduction & Historical Background**  
The term originates from the 18th and 19th centuries, particularly in Danbury, Connecticut (USA) and the UK. Hat makers used **mercuric nitrate** to treat animal fur for felt hats. Chronic inhalation of these mercury vapors led to a cluster of neurological symptoms known as the **"Mad Hatter Syndrome"**.

**Mechanism of Action**  
Mercury binds with **sulfhydryl (-SH) groups**, resulting in enzyme inhibition and pathological alteration of cellular membranes, primarily affecting the Central Nervous System (CNS).

---

#### **Clinical Features (The Progression of Shakes)**

The tremors are **coarse** and interspersed with **jerky movements** every few minutes. They are "intention tremors," meaning they worsen when the patient attempts to perform a voluntary task.

**Order of Appearance (Proximo-distal pattern):**

1. **Hands:** Starts as a fine tremor in the fingers, making it difficult to write legibly or dress (buttoning clothes).
2. **Face:** Progresses to involve the **lips and tongue**.
3. **Extremities:** Finally involves the **arms and legs**.

★ **Important Exam Point: Concussion Mercurilis**  
In the most advanced and severe stages, the tremors become so violent that the condition is referred to as **==*concussion mercurilis*==**.

---

#### **Associated Features: The Mercurial Triad**

Chronic mercury poisoning is diagnosed by a classic triad, of which Hatter's Shakes is a key component:

1. **Hatter’s Shakes** (Tremors).
2. **Mercurial Erethism:** Neuropsychiatric changes like extreme shyness, irritability, "frequent blushing," and explosive anger.
3. **Gingivostomatitis:** Inflamed gums, excessive salivation (ptyalism), and a **bluish-gray line** on the lower jaw.

---

#### **Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Occupational Hazard:** Identifying these shakes is crucial for diagnosing toxicity in workers in thermometer factories, mirror manufacturing, and glass-blowing industries.
- **Criminal Investigation:** Helps differentiate between intentional poisoning and accidental occupational exposure.
- **Expert Testimony:** Doctors must be able to distinguish these from other tremors (e.g., Parkinson’s or tremors from alcohol withdrawal).

#### **Management**

1. **Removal from Source:** Immediate discontinuation of mercury exposure.
2. **Specific Antidote (Chelation):**
    - **N-acetyl penicillamine:** The chelator of choice for **chronic** poisoning.
    - **DMSA (Succimer):** Also used to increase mercury excretion.
3. **Oral Hygiene:** Vital for managing gingivostomatitis.

---

#### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- **Mnemonic for Chronic Mercury (HG-MERCURI):** **H**atter's shakes, **G**ingivostomatitis, **M**ercurialentis, **E**rethism, **R**enal changes, **C**erebral palsy (organic), **U**rticaria, **R**ashes (Pink disease), **I**ntention tremors.
- **Mercurialentis:** A unique malt-brown reflex seen on the anterior lens capsule of the eye.
- **Acrodynia (Pink Disease):** An allergic reaction to mercury seen in children, presenting with pinkish rashes and painful extremities.

#### **Viva Questions**

1. **Why is it called Danbury Tremor?**
    - _Ans:_ Because Danbury, Connecticut, was a major center for hat manufacturing where many workers suffered from mercury toxicity.
2. **How do you differentiate the "Blue Line" on gums in Mercury vs. Lead?**
    - _Ans:_ In Lead, it is called the **Burtonian Line**. Both cause a blue/gray line, but mercury is more often associated with heavy salivation (ptyalism).
3. **Which chelator is used for Acute vs. Chronic Mercury?**
    - _Ans:_ **BAL (Dimercaprol)** is used for acute ingestion, while **N-acetyl penicillamine** is preferred for chronic cases.

#### **Quick Revision Summary**

- **Cause:** Chronic mercury vapor inhalation (Hydrargyrism).
- **Pattern:** Hands $\rightarrow$ Lips/Tongue $\rightarrow$ Arms/Legs.
- **Nature:** Intention tremor; coarse and jerky.
- **Key Law:** Section 33 BNSS (Reporting) and Section 123 BNS (Hurt).
- **Treatment:** N-acetyl penicillamine.
- **History:** Mad Hatters (Mercuric nitrate in felt hats).
