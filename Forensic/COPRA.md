[[Laws]]
### **Consumer Protection Act (COPRA)**

---

#### **Basic Concepts**

**Definition** The **Consumer Protection Act (COPRA)** is a social welfare legislation designed to protect the interests of consumers and provide a simple, fast, and inexpensive mechanism for the redressal of their grievances.

**Introduction and Historical Background**

- **Old Act:** Originally enacted in **1986** to provide a platform for consumers to sue for "deficiency in service" or "defects in goods."
- **New Act (Amendment):** The **Consumer Protection Act, 2019** was enacted to replace the 1986 Act, addressing modern challenges like e-commerce and telemarketing.
- **Effective Date:** The 2019 Act came into force on **July 20, 2020**.

**Objectives**

- To provide better protection of the interests of consumers.
- To establish authorities for timely and effective administration and settlement of consumer disputes.

**Importance in Medico-Legal Practice** In the landmark case of _Indian Medical Association vs. V.P. Shantha (1995)_, the Supreme Court ruled that medical services provided for a fee fall under the ambit of "service" as defined by COPRA. This means patients are considered "consumers," and they can sue doctors/hospitals for **deficiency in service** (medical negligence) in Consumer Redressal Commissions.

---

#### **Medico-Legal Importance**

- **Civil Liability:** COPRA deals with the civil liability of a medical practitioner. The focus is on **compensation** rather than punishment.
- **Role in Court Proceedings:** Cases are tried in specialized Consumer Commissions (District, State, or National levels) instead of traditional civil courts, leading to faster settlements.
- **Importance for Medical Practitioners:** Doctors must maintain meticulous medical records (indoor records for 3 years, MLC/registers for 10 years) as they serve as primary documentary evidence in COPRA cases.

★ **Important Exam Point:** Under COPRA, the burden of proof initially lies on the patient (complainant) to prove negligence, except in cases where the doctrine of _Res Ipsa Loquitur_ (the thing speaks for itself) applies.

---

#### **Relevant Law (2019 Amendments)**

The 2019 Act introduced several significant changes compared to the 1986 version:

|**Feature**|**Consumer Protection Act, 1986**|**Consumer Protection Act, 2019**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Pecuniary Jurisdiction**|Much lower limits.|Significantly increased limits for compensation.|
|**E-filing**|Not available.|Complaints can be filed **electronically**.|
|**Territorial Jurisdiction**|Where the opposite party resides/works.|Where the **complainant (patient)** resides or works.|
|**Mediation**|No legal provision.|Courts can refer cases for **settlement through mediation**.|
|**E-commerce**|Not covered.|Includes all online transactions and telemarketing.|

---

#### **Hierarchy of Consumer Redressal Commissions**

★ **High-Yield Exam Point: The 2019 Pecuniary Limits**

|**Commission Level**|**Compensation Amount (Pecuniary Limit)**|**Appeal Process**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**District Commission**|Up to **₹1 Crore**|Appeal to State Commission within **45 days**.|
|**State Commission**|**₹1 Crore to ₹10 Crore**|Appeal to National Commission within **30 days**.|
|**National Commission**|Above **₹10 Crore**|Appeal to the **Supreme Court**.|

---

#### **Rights of a Consumer (Patient)**

Under the Act, every consumer has six fundamental rights:

1. **Right to Safety:** Protection against hazardous services.
2. **Right to Information:** About the quality, potency, and price of services.
3. **Right to Choose:** Access to a variety of services at competitive prices.
4. **Right to be Heard:** Assurance that consumer interests will receive due consideration.
5. **Right to Redressal:** Against unfair trade practices or exploitation.
6. **Right to Consumer Education:** To be an informed consumer.

#### **Relevant Legal Concepts (BNS & BNSS)**

|**Concept**|**Definition**|**Relevant Provision**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Medical Negligence**|Absence of reasonable skill and care leading to damage.|**Section 106 BNS:** Punishment for causing death by negligence (up to 2 years).|
|**Civil vs. Criminal**|COPRA handles **Civil Negligence** (compensation). BNS handles **Criminal Negligence** (imprisonment).|**Section 106 BNS** (Criminal) vs. **COPRA 2019** (Civil).|
|**Medical Records**|Mandatory documentation to defend against COPRA claims.|**NMC Guidelines:** Records must be provided within **72 hours** of request.|

---

#### **Procedural Time Limits**

- **Filing a Complaint:** Must be filed within **2 years** from the date the cause of action (negligence) occurred.
- **Case Disposal:** Ideally, cases should be decided within **3 months**. If lab testing/analysis is required, the limit is **5 months**.

---

#### **High-Yield Exam Points**

- ★ **Effective Date:** July 20, 2020.
- ★ **District Commission Limit:** Up to ₹1 Crore (frequently confused with the old limit of 20 lakhs).
- ★ **Jurisdiction:** The patient can now sue from their home town.
- ★ **Medical Negligence:** Is a "deficiency in service" under COPRA.

---

#### **Viva Questions**

1. **Is a patient a consumer?**
    - _Ans:_ Yes, if they pay for the service. Patients treated for free in government hospitals are NOT consumers under COPRA.
2. **What is the 'Time Limitation' to file a case?**
    - _Ans:_ 2 years from the date of the incident.
3. **Can a doctor be imprisoned under COPRA?**
    - _Ans:_ No. COPRA provides civil remedies (compensation). Imprisonment for negligence occurs under **Section 106 BNS**.

#### **Quick Revision Summary (One-Page)**

- **Act:** COPRA 2019 (replaces 1986).
- **Enforcement:** 20 July 2020.
- **Patient Status:** Consumer (if payment is made).
- **Legal Claim:** "Deficiency in Service."
- **3-Tier System:** District (<1 Cr), State (1–10 Cr), National (>10 Cr).
- **New Features:** E-filing, Mediation, Video conferencing, Consumer's location jurisdiction.
- **Doctor's Defense:** High-quality **Medical Records** and showing that "Standard Care" was followed.
