### **Acute Arsenic Poisoning**
[[Metallic poisoning]]
---

#### **1. Basic Concepts**

**Introduction**  
Arsenic is a heavy metal known historically as the **"King of Poisons"** or the **"Inheritance Powder"** because it was frequently used to kill family members to gain property. Metallic arsenic is non-poisonous because it is not absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract (GIT); however, its compounds are highly toxic.

**Common Toxic Compounds**

- **Arsenious oxide (Arsenic trioxide, $As_2O_3$):** Also known as _sankhya_ or white arsenic. This is the **most toxic form**. It is a white, tasteless, and odorless powder, making it an ideal homicidal agent as it cannot be detected when mixed with food.
- **Copper Arsenite (Scheele's green):** Used as a coloring agent.
- **Arsenic Sulfide:** Yellow orpiment (_hartal_).
- **Arsine ($AsH_3$):** A highly toxic, colorless gas with a garlic-like odor.

**Mechanism of Action (Toxicodynamics)**  
Arsenic interferes with cellular respiration by uncoupling mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation. It binds with the **sulfhydryl (-SH) groups** of mitochondrial enzymes (specifically pyruvate dehydrogenase), inhibiting the citric acid cycle and decreasing ATP production.

---

#### **2. Medico-Legal Importance**

★ **Important Exam Point: Ideal Homicidal Poison**  
Arsenic is considered a classic homicidal poison for several reasons:

- It is cheap, easily available, tasteless, odorless, and colorless.
- The symptoms of acute poisoning closely **resemble natural disease** (specifically Cholera), allowing the offender to escape suspicion.
- Small quantities are sufficient to cause death.
- **Disadvantage for the criminal:** It retards putrefaction and can be detected in decomposed bodies, bones, hair, and nails for several years.

**Other Medico-Legal Aspects:**

- **Accidental:** Due to contaminated food, water (shallow tube wells), or improper medicinal use.
- **Abortifacient:** Applied locally as a paste on "abortion sticks" to procure criminal abortion.
- **Cattle Poison:** Used to kill livestock for hide or revenge.

#### **4. Clinical Features**

Acute poisoning manifests in three clinical forms based on the dose and route:

**I. Gastroenteric (GIT) Type (Commonest)**

- **Onset:** Symptoms appear within 10 minutes to 1 hour.
- **Symptoms:** Sweetish metallic taste, burning pain in the mouth/throat, and intense thirst.
- **Vomiting:** Persistent; initially food, then mucus, bile, and blood.
- **Purging:** Severe diarrhea that eventually becomes **rice-watery** (resembling cholera), sometimes containing blood.
- **Tenesmus:** Persistent and painful desire to evacuate the bowel.

**II. Fulminant Type**

- Occurs with a massive dose (>3 g).
- GIT symptoms are often absent.
- The patient collapses rapidly due to shock and peripheral vascular failure, with death occurring in 1–3 hours.

**III. Narcotic Type**

- GIT symptoms are less prominent.
- Characterized by giddiness, formication (crawling sensation on skin), delirium, coma, and death.

#### **5. Differential Diagnosis: Arsenic Poisoning vs. Cholera**

★ **Important Exam Point:** This comparison is a frequent university question.

|**Feature**|**Arsenic Poisoning**|**Cholera**|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Pain in Throat**|Occurs **before** vomiting/purging.|Occurs **after** vomiting/purging.|
|**Vomitus**|Contains mucus, bile, and blood.|Watery; no mucus or blood.|
|**Stools**|Rice-watery; may contain blood.|Rice-watery; no blood; passed in a "jet".|
|**Tenesmus**|**Present**.|Absent.|
|**Conjunctiva**|Inflamed/Red.|Not inflamed.|
|**Bacteriology**|No organisms.|_Vibrio cholerae_ present in culture.|

#### **6. Management**

1. **Hemodynamic Stabilization:** Primary importance due to massive fluid loss from vomiting and diarrhea.
2. **Gastric Decontamination:**
    - **Gastric Lavage:** Repeatedly with large amounts of warm water or milk.
    - ★ **Note:** Activated charcoal does **not** adsorb arsenic well and is not routinely recommended unless co-ingestants are suspected.
3. **Specific Antidote (Chelation Therapy):**
    - **BAL (British Anti-Lewisite/Dimercaprol):** 3–5 mg/kg IM every 4 hours for 2 days, then tapered.
    - **DMSA (Succimer) or DMPS:** Orally effective alternatives to BAL.
4. **Supportive Care:** IV fluids (Glucose-saline) to combat shock, and morphine for pain relief.

---

#### **7. Postmortem Findings (Autopsy)**

**External:**

- Body looks emaciated due to dehydration.
- Rigor mortis appears early.
- Putrefaction is **delayed** due to the anti-bacterial action of arsenic.

**Internal:**

- **Stomach:** Characteristically shows a **"Red Velvety" appearance**—mucosa is swollen, red, and covered with sticky mucus containing white particles of arsenic.
- **Heart:** **Subendocardial petechial hemorrhages** in the left ventricle are a classic sign.
- **Liver:** Fatty infiltration or acute necrosis.
- **Kidneys:** Acute tubular necrosis.

---

#### **8. Specimen Collection**

- **Preferred Specimen (Acute):** **Urine** is the best sample for confirming recent exposure.
- **Other Samples:** Stomach contents, liver, kidney, blood, and hair/nails (even in acute cases, as arsenic is excreted in hair within hours).

- ★ **Inheritance Powder:** Historical name for arsenic.
- ★ **Rice-water stools:** Characteristic of the GI type.
- ★ **Red Velvety Stomach:** Classic autopsy finding.
- ★ **Subendocardial Hemorrhage:** Heart finding in arsenic, barium, and phosphorus poisoning.
- ★ **BAL:** The traditional specific antidote.

### **Quick Revision Summary**

1. **Form:** Arsenic Trioxide ($As_2O_3$) is the most toxic homicidal form.
2. **Mechanism:** Inhibits cellular enzymes by binding -SH groups.
3. **Signs:** Mimics Cholera (Vomiting $\rightarrow$ Purging $\rightarrow$ Tenesmus).
4. **Treatment:** Stabilization + Lavage + BAL (Chelator).
5. **Autopsy:** Early rigor, delayed putrefaction, red velvety stomach, subendocardial hemorrhages.
6. **Law:** Duty to report to police under **Section 33 BNSS**.
